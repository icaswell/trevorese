Grammar 4: focus on various more specific things that crop up in English
“Whether”

“Whether” is a funny word in English. Basically, it just introduces a clause that is a yes-no question. Sesowi, like Chinese, does this much more simply.

en gloss
Do you know whether that’s a dog? you do hard-think this be big-good what?
He asked whether it was a dog this back do what-talk this be big-good what?
Animacy

Many languages treat nouns differently depending on whether they are animate (e.g. animals) or not (e.g. rocks), and may even have different articles. English uses the animate/inanimate distinction for some words, e.g. who vs what, him vs it, someone vs. something, young vs new. Sesowi does not make this distinction in any cases, but if clarity is needed, thing or person can be added in:

en gloss
what/whom do you see? you do see what?
what do you see? (inanimate) you do see what thing?
whom do you see? (animate) you do see what person?
a young person wears a new hat person like new do body-have head-surface like new
“It” as “that”

A special case of the impersonal are expressions like “it is a pig!”, when “it” basically means the same thing as “that” or “this”. Even in English, this is more or less the same as saying “This is a pig!”.

en loose gloss
What’s that? It’s a pig! <What is that? That’s a pig!> that do be what? that do be love-dirt-animal!
Who’s at the door? It’s your mom! <What is at door? This is your mom> what person do be place container-opening? this do be female-big of you!
It’s a boy! <This is a boy!> this do be male!
“It”/“you”/“They”: “People”

They say that this “passive”/“impersonal” phenomenon is a deep conspiracy that cuts across all pronouns. It’s everywhere. You can’t get away from it. Whether one recognizes it or not, the spectrum of impersonal phenomena extends deep into the language…or so it is said.

In reality, you can just translate these however you want. Just listing them here for completeness:

  • It is generally believed that X
  • you never know what’s going to happen
  • You know what they say
  • People are worried
Yes and no

Sesowi has one word for yes and one word for no:

en gloss
Are you happy? you like good-energy what?
yes. truth
no. no

These are the same as the words for “correct” and “incorrect”:

en gloss
Are you happy? you like good-energy what?
correct. truth
incorrect. no

English speakers or Spanish speakers may be confused as to why this gets its own section. How complex can “yes” and “no” be? However, “yes” and “no” are by no means a common phenomenon in language. Latin, Irish, Chinese, Toki Pona, and many others have no word for yes or no: they just repeat the verb. Hence why you see in Hiberno English, whose substrate is irish, exchanges like the following:

  • do you want tea?
  • I do. (Not “yes”)

There are also many languages with more than two words for yes or no. French uses the word si when saying yes and contradicting something. German uses the word doch to contradict the thing that was just said, so it can mean either yes or no depending on the context. Older English had four words: yea/nay/yes/no. Child English also has four: yes, no, yuh-huh, nuh-huh. (Note that these are different from “m-hm” and “m-m”, which are forms of “u-huh” and “uh-uh”, which simply mean “yes” and “no”.)

In Sesowi, if you want to emphasize that you are contradicting a statement, you can add against (“but”) before it:

en gloss
You are studying? you do get-think what?
yes. truth.
You are studying Chinese, right? you do get-think see-no-talk what?
but no! I’m studying Sesowi. [against] no! me do get-think talk-like-small.
Verbs with implied motion

In many languages, like English, many verbs are identical save for the directionality, like come/go/leave, bring/take. In Sesowi, there is only one verb for each, and if context is needed, direction and of clarify the direction.

en gloss
she came to my house this back do go direction big-container of me
she went to my house this back do go direction big-container of me
she came from my house this back do go of big-container of me
she left from my house this back do go of big-container of me
she carried an apple this back do arm-have hot-fruit
she brought an apple to the store this back do arm-have hot-fruit direction give-metal-place
she took an apple from the store this back do arm-have hot-fruit of give-metal-place
verbing nouns

In English, there are a variety of bespoke verbs to verbify different nouns. For instance, you play a violin, you do pushups, you sing a song. In Sesowi, these are all omitted. You just use the do particle to indicate that it is a verb:

en gloss
She plays piano this do piano
She does pushups this do number pushup
To have, to want, to try

These are verbs that are absent in many languages. Sesowi has them. We already talked about them.

too vs very contrast

Several languages, like Hindi and Chinese, don’t differentiate “too” and “very”. This seems like an odd failing. Sesowi distinguishes them. “Too” is over-more (“overmore”), whereas "very" is big ("big").

“one” and “some”

In English, we can use “one” to refer to something that has already been mentioned in conversation, or “some” to refer to a plural thing that has already been mentioned in conversation. In Sesowi, this is identical: one and number are used:

en gloss
I saw a bird. Did you also see one? me back do see air-animal. you other-thing back do see one what?
I saw one me back do see one
I saw some me back do see number
Alone, Only
en gloss
he is sitting alone this do down-body like one-person
he is only sitting this one-thing do down-body
I only talked to you me one-thing do talk direction you
I only want one thing me one-thing do want one thing
he washed himself alone this back do no-dirt self like one-person
Beyond this and that

Sometimes one needs to refer to two different subjects, and to differentiate them, you need to use “this” and “that”:

en gloss
which cat are you talking about? This one or that one? you do talk what cat? this other-thing that?

However, if you have more than two subjects, this gets rather confusing to deal with in English, and we usually say use emphasis. In Sesowi you can simply say “that-two”, “that-three”, and so on:

en gloss
I see this cat, that cat, the other cat, and the other other cat me do see this cat, that cat, that-two cat, that-three cat
this cat meowed at that cat, and then THAT cat meowed at that cat this cat back do cat-sound that cat, other that-time that-two cat back do cat-sound that cat
