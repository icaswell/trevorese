Grammar 2: more basics
Commands and yes/no questions

Questions and commands are formed by adding the particles what and with at the end of the sentence:

en gloss
You talk to the cat. you do talk direction cat
Do you talk to the cat? you do talk direction cat what?
Talk to the cat! you do talk direction cat with!
Let's talk to the cat! me-number do talk direction cat with!

Comparative Linguist's corner: this formation of questions is extremely common across the world's languages, from Singlish (ma) to Japanese (か), although it is not always the final position, as in Turkish "mi", which follows the predicate. Even English has this to some degree, e.g. "you're a bit big for this now, eh?".

As in most languages, you can leave off the "you"/ you for a command:

en gloss
Talk to the cat! do talk direction cat with!
Quantifiers and the wh- words

This what that we have been using to form questions is what is called (in Sesowi grammar) a quantifier. Quantifiers are little words that tell us something about the way that an object exists. They come before the noun. The quantifiers we have seen so far are this, that, what, number, no, and numbers, like one or 67.

There is one more of these fundamental quantifiers, namely all, meaning all/every/each or complete:

en gloss
You know everything you do hard-think all thing.
You like all cats you do love all cat
You are like all cats you like all cat

Two more words that fall in this category in English are some and any. For the most part these are entirely unnecessary in Sesowi:

en gloss
Someone is coming!
Anyone is coming!
A person is coming!
 person do this-go!
I want to live somewhere warm.
I want to live anywhere warm.
I want to live in a warm place.
 me do want life place like middle-hot
You say something
You say anything
You say a thing
 you do talk thing.

However, if you need to stress the non-specificness of something, you can use all-possibility:

en gloss
Which cat do you talk to? you do talk direction what cat?
Oh, just some [nonspecific] cat that, self cat like all-possibility.

With these in our arsenal, we can now express an amazing range of things. Let's introduce some more words:

en gloss
place place
thing thing
time time
reason reason
way way
amount/number number
person person

These words are very powerful when combined with the quantifiers ! Let's see what happens when we combine them with the question word what:

en gloss
which? / what? what?
where? what place?
what? what thing?
when? what time?
why? what reason?
how? what way?
how much? what number?
who? what person?

How might you answer these questions?

en gloss
here / there / somewhere this place / that place / place (like all-possibility)
this / that / something this thing / that thing / thing (like all-possibility)
now / then / sometime this time / that time / time (like all-possibility)
because (for this reason) / because (for that reason) / just because (for some reason) this reason / that reason / reason (like all-possibility)
this much / that much / some amount this number / that number / number (like all-possibility)
thus (in this way) / thus (in that way) / somehow this way / that way / way (like all-possibility)
this person / that person / someone this person / that person / person (like all-possibility)

Similarly, with the universal quantifier:

en gloss
everywhere all place
everything all thing
every time / all the time all time
for every reason all reason
?? all number
?? all way
everyone all person

Look closely at these. In English, we have so many different words for these concepts, and it hides how similar they all are! It is common for a language to be like English in this way, and have some collection of concepts from different roots and languages, such that the true structure is hidden. However, several other languages actually make these relations perfectly clear — for instance Telugu, what uses exactly the structure described above!

an adjective can be a whole sentence! (relative clauses)

Let's get back to adjectives. As you recall, adjectives are just formed by saying that a noun is like another noun:

en gloss
a cat cat
a black cat cat like dark-color
a little baby small-small-person like small

But what if you want to say that the cat is like something more complicated than a single noun? What if you want to say that the cat is like a little baby?

en gloss
a cat that is like a little baby cat like small-small-person like small

You can do that too! In fact, you can put a full sentence in front of the like, as long as it describes how that cat is:

en gloss
The cat I saw yesterday cat like me back do see (this) back-light-time
the cat that is meowing sadly cat like (this) do cat-sound like no-good-energy
the cat that is meowing sadly at me cat like (this) do cat-sound like no-good-energy direction me
the cat that I think is meowing sadly at me cat like me do think do cat-sound like no-good-energy direction me
the cat that I don't know where he is cat like me no do hard-think this do be what place

Note that according to the "proper" English grammar, this last example is impossible to express. As you can see, like is a very powerful tool! It is used anywhere English uses "that" to start a relative clause.

NB: The this is optional here; it is included in parentheses for completeness.

Comparative and Superlative

Comparison is done with the special comparison word more, meaning "more", and use direction to indicate the thing it is being compared to:

en gloss
That cat is sweet That cat like sugar
That cat is sweeter That cat like more sugar
That cat is sweeter than this cat That cat like more sugar direction this cat
My cat is bigger than your dog cat of me like more big direction big-good of you

To express less than you can use against-more, aka "anti-more"

en gloss
My cat is richer than your dog cat of me like more have-metal direction big-good of you
My cat is less rich than your dog cat of me like against-more have-metal direction big-good of you

Let's introduce two more words: one-way (same) and other-way (different). This allows us to compare things that are the same or different, without having to specify if it is more or less:

en gloss
My cat is the same as your cat cat of me like one-way big direction cat of you
My cat is the same size as your cat cat of me like one-way big direction cat of you
My cat is different from your cat cat of me like other-way big direction cat of you
My cat is a different size than your cat cat of me like other-way big direction cat of you

In English, these comparisons use all sorts of different structures and prepositions to express these concepts. But Sesowi treats them all the same way: simple comparisons with direction.

Consider the following argument between two people about which cat is sweeter:

en gloss
this cat is sweet this cat like sugar
Yes, he is very sweet truth, this like big sugar
But that cat is sweeter than this cat against that cat like more sugar direction this cat
That cat is sweeter that cat like more sugar
This cat is less sweet than that cat this cat like against-more sugar direction that cat
That cat is much sweeter than this cat that cat like big more sugar direction this cat
This cat is much less sweet than that cat this cat like big against-more sugar direction that cat

Hopefully, they will eventually reach a conclusion like the following:

en gloss
That cat is as sweet as this cat that cat like one-way sugar direction this cat

On the other hand, they might start using the superlative. To form the superlative, use over.:

en loose gloss
That cat is the sweetest That cat is top sweet / peak sweet that cat like [over more] sugar
That cat is the least sweet That cat is bottom sweet that cat like over [more] sugar
Verbs 3: Verbs acting on other verbs: tenses and modals

We have seen how to use a verb with a noun as its object:
normal verb: I see a dog (the object of the verb see is the noun dog)
Now let's talk about verbs that act on other verbs, which we can call "modal verbs":
modal verb: I can sing (the object of the modal verb can is the verbsing,)

The most common verbs that act on other verbs are a set of five or so verbs that actually can ONLY1 take verbs as objects. For instance, I can sing works, but I can the cat has no meaning. These obligate modals almost always work in funny and particular ways in a language.2 Here is their use in Sesowi:

en gloss
the cat can (is able to) talk cat can do talk
the cat may (is allowed to) talk cat can do talk
the cat should (ought to) talk cat must do talk
the cat must (has to/needs to) talk cat must do talk
the cat might (possibly will) talk cat possibility do talk

Note that in Sesowi, as in English, can can mean both "can" and "may", and must can mean "should" or "must".

Then there are a lot of other verbs that can act on verbs OR nouns. In English/German/etc., these are treated differently; in Sesowi they are the same as the so-called "true modals":

en gloss
the cat wants to talk cat want do talk
the cat tries to talk cat seek do talk
the cat likes to talk cat love do talk

Verbs that act on other verbs are incredibly powerful, and capture all the different ways you can perform a noun – including tense! When we told you about tenses before, we only told you half the story. What we didn't tell you is that the two tense markers we taught you — back and front — are in fact themselves just a special case of verbs acting on verbs. Observe:

en gloss
the cat is talking
the cat talks		 cat do talk
the cat did talk
the cat talked		 cat back do talk
the cat will talk
the cat is going to talk		 cat front do talk

All these constructions are all formed the same way! And indeed, in Sesowi, there is no difference between "tenses" and "modal verbs". The concept of a "tense" as it is usually conceived is based on how Indo-European languages work, where tenses are treated in a special way, but modals are treated more like verbs. However, in other languages, it's not so. In Chinese, Vietnamese, or Malay, tenses are expressed as adverbs, and in Telugu, tenses and modals are in the same category of suffixes!

Reminder: every verb has exactly one do. It sticks to the main verb like a prefix, and other things, like modals or tenses, line up before it without a do of their own.

multiple verbs acting on verbs

Want to have multiple verbs acting on another verb? No problem!

en gloss
the cat may be able to talk cat possibility can do talk
the cat will be able to talk cat front can do talk
the cat should have talked cat back must do talk
the cat should be able to talk cat must can do talk
the cat should not be able to talk cat no must can do talk
the cat should not have been able to talk cat no back must can do talk
the cat should not have been able to try to talk cat no back must can seek do talk

Does this last sentence look a bit extreme? It's really not! consider this dialogue:
A: "Hey, you said your organization was going to create a robot, right? How'd that go? Were you going to let it talk to other employees?"
B: "It went terribly. It should not have been allowed to be able to try to talk to others. We shouldn't have let it even have the capability to initiate conversation."

multiple verbs acting on verbs with adverbs in between

One small example should suffice to show how this is simpler in Sesowi:

en gloss
the cat should not be able to talk cat no must can do talk
the cat should not ever [even one time] be able to talk cat no must can do talk like one time

This is the same story for any verb that can take a noun or a verb as its object, like "try", "like", "need", etc.

More verbs acting on Verbs: the "Instrumental case"

Many languages have a special way of marking things when they are being used to do something. Sesowi, like Chinese, simply uses "use". It is just another case of a verb acting on another verb.

en gloss
He used a shovel to dig the hole. this back do use dirt-go-thing do dirt-go through-thing
He dug the hole with a shovel. this back do use dirt-go-thing do dirt-go through-thing

Note that the English "with" here is an instrumental marker in English, and actually a quite different meaning from the normal meaning. For the normal meaning of "with", this would mean "He and a shovel were digging a hole"!

Indirect objects

Some verbs have two objects. Consider the verbs "give" , "say", "talk", and "throw". They can have a single object:

en gloss
He gives a gift. this do give give-thing
He says a word this do talk talk-part
He talks about work this do talk of hard-do
He throws a rock this do no-link hard-thing
He steals a rock this do no-good-get hard-thing

However, you can also add a second object with a preposition like to or at. This second object is called the indirect object. Just as English, Sesowi marks these with the preposition "to" (aka direction)

en gloss
He gives a gift to me this do give give-thing direction me
He says a word to me this do talk talk-part direction me
He talks about work to me this do talk of hard-do direction me
He yells at me this do big-sound direction me
He throws a rock to me this do no-link hard-thing direction me
He steals a rock from me this do no-good-get hard-thing direction me
He puts bag on the table this do place container direction over flat-thing

When speaking, we need some way to differentiate between these two objects. For example, "give me to the girl" and "give me the girl" are very different sentences. To an English speaker, this should be pretty straightforward: you just indicate the recipient of the verb phrase with direction. Why is it worth mentioning? First of all, other languages treat indirect objects differently (e.g. with the Dative case). Second of all, English has another way of marking the indirect object, just by using the word order:

en gloss
He gives me a gift this do give give-thing direction me
He talks to me about work this do talk of hard-do direction me
He throws me a rock this do no-link hard-thing direction me

In Sesowi, you can never omit the preposition direction for the indirect object.

There are a few verbs in English that use "to" even when it appears to be a direct object. For instance, you talk to someone. However, in these cases this is because there is an implied indirect object: when you talk to someone, you are actually saying words (the direct object) to someone (the indirect object). The situation is the same for Sesowi: for these verbs with implied objects, we use the preposition direction:

en gloss
He writes a letter this do mark number-mark
He writes to me this do mark direction me
He writes a letter about pigs this do mark number-mark of love-dirt-animal
He writes a letter to me about pigs this do mark number-mark of love-dirt-animal direction me
Give [to] me the girl! do give female direction me with !
Give me to the girl! do give me direction female with!

This point should not pose problems for speakers of English: they should just translate "to" into direction. This should be familiar also to speakers of Chinese, where direction can be interpreted as 对: one can see that above, the treatment of to talk is the same as Chinese 说 vs 说对.

Note 1: In Chinese, there is the exact same phenomenon, where if there is no particle, the first object is the indirect object and the second is direct (I give you a thing / 我给你一个东西). if you invert the order of the objects but want the same meaning, you need to add a particle (I give a thing to you / 我把一个东西给你). However, instead of marking the indirect object, Chinese marks the direct object.

Note 2: Interestingly, in English there are also verbs where you are not allowed to use the word order, and you have to use a preposition. Consider the word say. While you can say Give me the cat, you can't say *Say me the thing.

Verbs 4: Infinitives and gerunds

In Indo-European languages like English, verbs have a special form called the infinitive, which is used in certain situations when there isn't a subject doing the verb. In English, you can often use the gerund (-ing form) in similar ways.

Many situations where IE languages use the infinitive is when you have a verb acting on another verb. We already saw how to do this:

en gloss
the cat wants the mouse to be here cat want tooth-animal do be this-place
She doesn't like walking / She doesn't like to walk this no love do arm-go
He thinks of talking this think do talk

Another situation where IE languages use an infinitive or gerund is when you are referring to the general state of a verb, as in "walking is good" or "It is good to walk." In this case, the verb has become a sort of noun, meaning "the state of walking". In Sesowi, we just need to add the "do" to show that it is a verb, and then you can use it like any other noun:

en gloss
Walking is good / It is good to walk. do arm-go like good.
He thinks talking is hard / it is hard to talk this do think do talk like no-easy
Being a student is fun / It is fun to be a student. do be get-think-person like play
You being here makes me happy. you do be this place do good-energy me .
The fact that you are here makes me happy. you do be this place do good-energy me.

Serendipitously, this ends up sounding similar to the English construction "to Verb"!

Finally, there are situations in English where gerunds like being pop up just because English uses "to be" for adjectives as well:

en gloss
He was being cute this back like can-love
She is being mean to me this like no-good direction me
Verbs 5: Clauses and conjunctions

The most basic way to make two clauses in Sesowi is to just put two sentences one after the other:

en gloss
I told you me back do talk direction you
the cat breaks a cup cat do break water-container
I told you (that) the cat broke a cup me back do talk direction you, cat do break water-container
In English, we can optionally put a "that" in there to make the causality clear. In Sesowi, as in Chinese, there is no such word and you just put the clauses next to each other.

Of course, you can also use conjunctions to connect two clauses:
en gloss
I told you, but the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, against cat do break water-container
I told you, and the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, other-thing cat do break water-container
I told you, because the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, this-reason cat do break water-container
I told you, so the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, give-reason cat do break water-container
I told you before the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, back-time cat do break water-container
I told you while/when the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, time cat do break water-container
I told you or the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, TODO cat do break water-container
I told you after the cat broke the cup me do talk direction you, front-time cat do break water-container
Serial Verb Constructions

To describe a sequence of actions by the same subject, multiple verbs can be "chained" or "serialized" one after another without any conjunctions. All verbs in the chain share the same subject and any preceding modals or tense markers.

en loose gloss
He stops, turns his head, and drinks. <He stops, turns head, drinks.> this do no-go, do other-way head, do water-eat.
I want to go to the store, buy milk, and come back. <I want to go to store, buy milk, go back.> me want do go direction give-metal-place, do metal light-water, do go other-way.
You should go find him. <You must go, find him.> you must do go, do all-seek this.
She ran home and ate. <She past went-fast to house, ate.> this back do fast-go direction big-container, do eat.
other
"other" is the last of the quantifiers we haven't introduced:
en gloss
I saw a another cat me back do see other cat
The other cat was smaller other cat like more small
Self, own, each other
If the subject of a verb is the same as an object, you can use "self". It doesn't get tangled weirdly into verbs like Spanish "se" or German "sich". Using "self" doesn't change the meaning of the verb at all.
en gloss
he washed the towel this back do no-dirt water-flat-stuff
he washed himself this back do no-dirt self
they washed themselves this-number back do no-dirt self
The English word "own", in the sense of "their own", really means "of their self". It is expressed this way in Sesowi (as it is in Chinese):
en gloss
he ate his own feces this back do eat animal-dirt of self
she didn't know her own name this no back do hard-think self-talk of self
To express "each other", use "other self":
en gloss
they flog themselves this-number do link-energy-arm self
they flog each other this-number do link-energy-arm other self

Emphasis

English has a messy system of stressing a word with tone. Sesowi adopts the more common approach of adding a particle after a word to stress it, and this word is "self", much like how in English, we might say "he himself said so", or "he himself admitted himself that he suck".
en gloss
what did you say? you do talk what thing?
You said WHAT? you do talk what thing self?
YOU said what? you self do talk what thing?
even you said that. self you do talk that
you only said that. [Indian English] you self do talk that
you even said that you do talk self that
you said even that you do talk that self
This also ends up sounding like the emphatic clitics in Irish.
also, again
"Also" is a funny little word that basically just adds some emphasis. It's a regular adjective/adverb in Sesowi, like in English:
engloss
I also saw a cat
I too saw a cat
I saw a cat as well		me other-thing back do see cat
The cat is also small
The cat is small as well		cat other-thing like small
Again is also a straightforward adverb:
en gloss
I saw a cat again me other-time do see cat
