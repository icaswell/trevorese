Sesowi Lesson 1: The Basics of Being

Vocabulary

  • i: (quantifier/pronoun) this, he, she, it
  • a: (quantifier/pronoun) that, he, she, it
  • mi: (pronoun) I, me
  • yu: (pronoun) you
  • du: (verb marker) marks the following word as a verb
  • bi: (noun/verb) to be, existence
  • mang: (noun) person, human
  • boi: (noun) animal
  • mau: (noun) cat
  • ting: (noun) thing
  • no: (quantifier/particle) no, not

Grammar: Simple Sentences with "to be"

The most basic sentence in Sesowi identifies something. The structure is Subject + du bi + Object. The word du is a special marker that tells you the next word is a verb. bi is the verb "to be".

To negate a sentence, you simply add no before the verb marker du.

  • i du bi mau. - This is a cat.
  • mi du bi mang. - I am a person.
  • a no du bi boi. - That is not an animal.

Story: mi du bi mang

mi du bi mang. yu du bi mang. i du bi mau. a du bi boi.

i no du bi mang. a no du bi ting.

mi du bi mi. yu du bi yu.

(I am a person. You are a person. This is a cat. That is an animal.)

(This is not a person. That is not a thing.)

(I am me. You are you.)

Sesowi Lesson 2: Describing Things

Vocabulary

  • so: (particle) marks the following word as an adjective/modifier
  • da: (noun/adj) big, very
  • wi: (noun/adj) small
  • ga: (noun/adj) good
  • ni: (noun/adj) dark, blackness
  • bai: (noun/adj) light, white, bright
  • ma: (noun/verb) love, to like/love

  • noga: (compound: no-ga) bad
  • niyang: (compound: ni-yang) black (color) (atom yang (color) is implied)
  • baiyang: (compound: bai-yang) white (color)
  • daga: (compound: da-ga) dog (lit. "big-good")

Grammar: Adjectives with "so"

In Sesowi, there are no separate "adjectives". To describe something, you say it is "like" another noun using the particle so. The structure is Noun + so + Descriptor. This can be a simple description or a full "is" sentence.

  • mau so ni. - A black cat / The cat is black.
  • i daga so da. - This dog is big.
  • yu so ga. - You are good.

Story: mau so da, mau so wi

mi du si daga. i daga so da so baiyang. i daga so ga.

yu du si mau. a mau so wi so niyang. yu du ma a mau. a mau so noga e?

no. a mau no so noga. a mau so ga.

(I see a dog. This dog is big and white. This dog is good.)

(You see a cat. That cat is small and black. You like that cat. Is that cat bad?)

(No. That cat is not bad. That cat is good.)

Sesowi Lesson 3: Taking Action

Vocabulary

  • se: (noun/verb) talk, speech; to talk/say
  • ka: (noun/verb) eat
  • si: (noun/verb) see, sight
  • go: (noun/verb) go, a trip
  • lo: (noun/adj) water, wet
  • gu: (noun) stuff, material

  • kagu: (compound: ka-gu) food
  • loka: (compound: lo-ka) to drink (lit. "water-eat")
  • logo: (compound: lo-go) to swim (lit. "water-go")
  • boigu: (compound: boi-gu) meat

Grammar: Verbs with "du"

We've used du bi for "to be". Any noun can be turned into a verb by putting du in front of it. This is like saying you "do" the noun.

  • mi du se. - I talk / I am talking.
  • daga du ka. - The dog eats.
  • yu du si mau. - You see the cat. (The object comes after the verb).

Story: daga du ka

mi du si daga. daga du ka boigu.

yu du si mau. mau du loka lo.

daga du go. mau du go.

mi du si daga. yu du si mau. daga ba mau du go.

(I see a dog. The dog eats meat.)

(You see a cat. The cat drinks water.)

(The dog goes. The cat goes.)

(I see the dog. You see the cat. The dog and the cat go.) (Note: ba for "and" is a preview!)

Sesowi Lesson 4: Possession and Place

Vocabulary

  • de: (preposition) of, from, about
  • tu: (preposition) to, towards
  • bau: (noun) container, box
  • lu: (noun/verb) place; to put
  • ne: (preposition/noun) in, inside

  • dabau: (compound: da-bau) house (lit. "big container")
  • mamang: (compound: ma-mang) friend (lit. "love person")
  • golu: (compound: go-lu) road, path (lit. "go place")
  • ilu: (compound: i-lu) here (lit. "this place")
  • alu: (compound: a-lu) there (lit. "that place")

Grammar: Possession with "de"

To show possession, Sesowi uses the word de, similar to "of" in English. The structure is Thing + de + Owner.

  • mau de mi. - My cat (the cat of me).
  • dabau de yu. - Your house.
  • mamang de mau. - The cat's friend.

Story: dabau de mi

i du bi dabau de mi. i dabau so da.

mamang de mi du bi ne dabau.

mi du se tu mamang de mi.

mau de mamang du go de dabau tu golu.

a mau du go alu.

(This is my house. This house is big.)

(My friend is in the house.)

(I talk to my friend.)

(The friend's cat goes from the house to the road.)

(That cat goes there.)

Sesowi Lesson 5: Past and Future

Vocabulary

  • ya: (tense marker) marks past tense; back
  • be: (tense marker) marks future tense; front
  • tai: (noun) time
  • meng: (noun/verb) thought, mind; to think
  • kumeng: (compound: ku-meng) to know (lit. "hard-think")
  • baitai: (compound: bai-tai) day (lit. "light-time")
  • nitai: (compound: ni-tai) night (lit. "dark-time")
  • yabaitai: (compound: ya-baitai) yesterday (lit. "back-day")
  • bebaitai: (compound: be-baitai) tomorrow (lit. "front-day")
  • itai: (compound: i-tai) now (lit. "this-time")
  • atai: (compound: a-tai) then (lit. "that-time")

Grammar: Tense Markers

Tense is simple in Sesowi. You add a tense marker right before the verb marker du.

  • Present: mi du se. - I talk.
  • Past: mi ya du se. - I talked. (ya means back/past)
  • Future: mi be du se. - I will talk. (be means front/future)

Story: yabaitai, bebaitai

yabaitai, mi ya du si daga. i ya du bi daga so ga.

bebaitai, mi be du si a daga. mi du meng i be du go tu dabau de mi.

itai, i daga du bi ilu. i du loka lo.

mi du kumeng i du bi mamang de mi.

(Yesterday, I saw a dog. It was a good dog.)

(Tomorrow, I will see that dog. I think it will go to my house.)

(Now, this dog is here. It is drinking water.)

(I know it is my friend.)

Sesowi Lesson 6: Questions and Commands

Vocabulary

  • e: (quantifier/particle) what, which; marks a yes/no question at the end of a sentence
  • ba: (particle) marks a command at the end of a sentence; please
  • dwe: (noun/interjection) truth, correctness; yes
  • yo: (noun/verb) possession; to have
  • ge: (noun/verb) gift; to give
  • pu: (noun/verb) get, obtain
  • geting: (compound: ge-ting) gift (lit. "give-thing")
  • kudu: (compound: ku-du) work, job (lit. "hard-do")
  • yau: (noun/verb) want, desire; to want
  • na: (quantifier/noun) plural marker; number, amount, group
  • ina: (compound: i-na) they, them
  • mina: (compound: mi-na) we, us
  • yuna: (compound: yu-na) you (plural), you all

Grammar: Questions, Commands, and Plurals

  1. Yes/No Questions: Add e to the end of a statement.
    • yu du ma daga e? - Do you like dogs?
  2. Commands: Add ba to the end of a statement. You can drop the subject (yu).
    • (yu) du go ba! - Go!
  3. Plurals: Add the quantifier na before a noun to make it unambiguously plural. Without it, a noun can be singular or plural.
    • na daga - dogs (plural)

Story: du ge kagu ba!

na mau de mi du yau kagu. ina du se tu mi.

mi du se tu ina, "yuna du yau kagu e?"

ina du se, "dwe!"

mi du se, "mina be du go pu kagu."

wa mau so da du se, "du ge kagu tu mi ba!"

mi ya du ge kagu tu na mau. itai, ina so ga.

(My cats want food. They talk to me.)

(I say to them, "Do you all want food?")

(They say, "Yes!")

(I say, "We will go get food.")

(One big cat says, "Give food to me!")

(I gave food to the cats. Now, they are good.)

Sesowi Lesson 7: Who, What, Where

Vocabulary

  • wai: (noun) reason
  • we: (noun) way, method
  • ko: (noun) arm, limb, branch
  • pau: (noun/verb) end; to end/finish
  • pi: (noun) surface, skin

  • e mang?: who?
  • e ting?: what?
  • e lu?: where?
  • e tai?: when?
  • e wai?: why?
  • e we?: how?
  • kopau: (compound: ko-pau) hand (lit. "arm-end")
  • topi: (compound: to-pi) hat (atom to (head) is implied)

Grammar: "Wh-" Questions

Questions like "who, what, where" are formed by combining the question word e with a general noun like mang (person), lu (place), or ting (thing). These can be placed wherever the answer would go in a sentence.

  • e mang du bi ilu? - Who is here?
  • yu du si e ting? - What do you see?
  • dabau de yu du bi e lu? - Where is your house?

Story: e lu?

yabaitai, mi ya du yo topi so noga.

itai, mi no du yo topi. topi de mi du bi e lu?

mi du se tu mamang de mi, "yu ya du si topi de mi e?"

i du se, "no, mi no ya du si."

e mang ya du pu topi de mi? e wai?

a! mi du si i! daga de mi du yo topi de mi!

i du meng i topi du bi ting so ga du ka!

(Yesterday, I had a bad hat.)

(Now, I don't have the hat. Where is my hat?)

(I say to my friend, "Did you see my hat?")

(She says, "No, I did not see it.")

(Who took my hat? Why?)

(Ah! I see it! My dog has my hat!)

(He thinks the hat is a good thing to eat!)

Sesowi Lesson 8: More actions and descriptions

Vocabulary

  • subo: (verb) to sit (down-body)
  • kogo: (verb) to walk (leg-go/arm-go)
  • bu: (verb) to seek, to try
  • lang: (adj) long
  • li: (noun) energy, feeling
  • gali: (adj) happy (good-energy)
  • nogali: (adj) sad, hurt (not-good-energy)
  • langboi: (compound: lang-boi) snake
  • goko: (compound: go-ko) leg
  • siting: (compound: si-ting) camera (a preview, not used in story)

Grammar: Adverbs with "so"

Adverbs work just like adjectives. You place the action (verb) first, then add so and the describing noun. This describes *how* the action was done.

  • Adjective: daga so gali. - The happy dog.
  • Adverb: daga du se so gali. - The dog talks happily.
  • Adverb: mi du kogo so noga. - I walk badly.

Story: langboi so nogali

yabaitai mi ya du kogo ne golu so lang.

mi ya du si langboi. i langboi so wi.

i ya du subo. i ya so nogali.

mi ya du se tu i so ga. "e wai yu so nogali?"

i langboi ya du se tu mi, "mi du bu mamang de mi. i du bi e lu?"

mi no ya du kumeng. mi ya so nogali laiting.

(Yesterday I walked on a long road.)

(I saw a snake. The snake was small.)

(It was sitting. It was sad.)

(I talked to it nicely. "Why are you sad?")

(The snake said to me, "I am seeking my friend. Where is he?")

(I did not know. I was sad also.)

Sesowi Lesson 9: Can, Must, Should

Vocabulary

  • nang: (modal verb) can, may (ability or permission)
  • dwa: (modal verb) must, should, ought to
  • po: (modal verb) might, possibly
  • kiboi: (compound: ki-boi) bird (air-animal) (atom ki (air) implied)
  • gasang: (noun/verb) music, song; to sing (good-sound) (atom sang (sound) implied)
  • sangsi: (verb) to hear, to listen (sound-see)
  • aikiboi: (compound: ai-kiboi) chicken (egg-bird) (atom ai (egg) implied)
  • kigo: (verb) to fly (air-go)
  • nangma: (adj) cute (can-love)
  • omeng: (verb) to decide (all-think) (atom o (all) implied)

Grammar: Modal Verbs

Modal verbs like "can," "must," and "might" come before the main verb construction. They do not take their own du. The structure is Subject + modal + du + Verb.

  • kiboi nang du kigo. - The bird can fly.
  • mi dwa du go. - I must go.
  • yu po du si i. - You might see it.

You can chain them: mi po nang du go. - I might be able to go.

Story: kiboi nang du kigo e?

aikiboi de mi du yau du kigo. i du se, "mi dwa du kigo!"

mi du se, "yu po nang du kigo, geng yu du bi aikiboi. na aikiboi no nang du kigo so da."

i aikiboi so nangma du subo so nogali.

betai, i du omeng. i be du bu du kigo!

i ya du go ta dabau. i ya du tau! i no ya du kigo. i ya du su!

i no so nogali. i du meng, "bebaitai, mi be nang du kigo!"

(My chicken wants to fly. It says, "I must fly!")

(I say, "You might be able to fly, but you are a chicken. Chickens cannot fly very much.")

(The cute chicken sits sadly.)

(Then, it decides. It will try to fly!)

(It went on top of the house. It jumped! It did not fly. It fell!)

(It is not sad. It thinks, "Tomorrow, I will be able to fly!")

Sesowi Lesson 10: Comparison

Vocabulary

  • mai: (adv) more
  • gengmai: (adv) less (against-more) (atom geng (against) implied)
  • ta: (preposition/adv) up, over, top
  • su: (preposition/adv) down, under, bottom
  • swi: (adj) sweet
  • dawe: (adj) important (big-way)
  • wawe: (adj) same (one-way)
  • laiwe: (adj) different (other-way) (atom lai (other) implied)
  • tating: (noun) top, superlative marker (over-thing)

Grammar: Comparatives and Superlatives

  1. Comparative (more/less): Use so mai [adj] tu [thing] (more ... than) or so gengmai [adj] tu [thing] (less ... than).
    • i mau so mai da tu a mau. - This cat is bigger than that cat.
  2. Superlative (most/least): Use so tating mai [adj] (the most...) or so tating gengmai [adj] (the least...).
    • i mau so tating da. - This cat is the biggest. (A simpler form `so ta mai [adj]` is also common).

Story: e mau so tating ga?

mi du yo do mau. wa mau so niyang, lai mau so baiyang.

mau so niyang so mai da tu mau so baiyang.

geng, mau so baiyang so mai kwi tu mau so niyang.

mamang de mi du ese, "e mau so mai ga?"

mi du se, "i omeng so ku. mau so niyang so mai mamang. mau so baiyang so mai se."

ina so laiwe, geng mi du ma ina so wawe.

o mau so tating ga!

(I have two cats. One cat is black, the other cat is white.)

(The black cat is bigger than the white cat.)

(But, the white cat is faster than the black cat.)

(My friend asks, "Which cat is better?")

(I say, "This is a hard decision. The black cat is more friendly. The white cat is more talkative.")

(They are different, but I love them the same.)

(All cats are the best!)

Sesowi Lesson 11: Complex Descriptions

Vocabulary

  • lelu: (noun) country, province
  • dapwalu: (noun) forest (tree-place)
  • koyo: (verb) to hold, grasp, carry
  • lapa: (noun) apple (hot-fruit)
  • swe: (noun) dirt, earth
  • loswe: (noun) mud (water-dirt)
  • goko: (noun) leg
  • subo: (verb) to sit (down-body)
  • nogali: (adj) sad, pained
  • damang: (noun) adult (big-person)
  • wimang: (noun) child (small-person)

Grammar: Relative Clauses with "so"

The word so is very powerful. It can connect a noun to a whole sentence that describes it. This is how you say "the cat that I saw" or "the person who is walking".

The structure is: Noun + so + [Describing Sentence].

  • mau so mi ya du si - The cat that I saw.
  • damang so du kogo - The adult who is walking.
  • i du bi lapa so ya du su. - This is an apple that fell.

Story: wimang so nogali

mi ya du kogo ne dapwalu. mi ya du si wimang so subo ne loswe. goko de i so noga.

mi ya du ese, "e wai yu subo ilu? yu so nogali e?"

i wimang so ya du koyo goko de i ya du se, "dwe, goko de mi so nogali."

i wimang so mi ya du si ya du yau du go tu dabau de i.

mi ya du koyo i wimang, mi ya du kogo tu dabau de i.

i damang so du bi muda de i ya so da gali.

(I was walking in the forest. I saw a child who was sitting in the mud. Their leg was hurt.)

(I asked, "Why are you sitting here? Are you hurt?")

(The child, who was holding their leg, said, "Yes, my leg hurts.")

(The child that I saw wanted to go to their house.)

(I carried the child, and I walked to their house.)

(The adult who is their mother was very happy.)

Sesowi Lesson 12: Using and Doing

Vocabulary

  • dai: (verb) to use
  • pwako: (noun) stick, branch
  • bwa: (noun) opening, mouth
  • swego: (verb) to dig (dirt-go)
  • twating: (noun) hole (that goes through)
  • badu: (verb) to mix (with-place)
  • badu: (noun) project (with-do)
  • duting: (noun) device, machine
  • kudu: (noun) work, job
  • pumeng: (verb) to study, learn
  • pumengmang: (noun) student

Grammar: The Instrumental Case with "dai"

In English, we say "I dug a hole with a shovel." The "with" shows what tool you used. In Sesowi, you use the verb dai (to use). The structure is more literal: Subject + du dai [Tool] + du [Action].

  • mi du dai pwako du swego. - I use a stick to dig.
  • i du dai duting du kudu. - He uses a machine to work.

This is a type of verb acting on another verb, similar to modals.

Story: badu de pumengmang

pumengmang so ga dwa du kudu so da.

i pumengmang du yo badu. i dwa du swego twating so da.

i nang du dai kopau de i, geng i be du so noga. i goko be so noga laiting.

gewai, i dwa du dai duting. i ya du bu duting so nang du swego.

i ya du obu i. i ya du dai duting du kudu.

i ya du swego twating so tating da! i ya so gali.

(A good student must work hard.)

(This student has a project. He must dig a big hole.)

(He can use his hands, but it will be bad. His back will hurt also.)

(Therefore, he must use a machine. He searched for a machine that can dig.)

(He found it. He used the machine to work.)

(He dug the biggest hole! He was happy.)

Sesowi Lesson 13: All and Every

Vocabulary

  • o: (quantifier) all, every, each
  • omang: (noun) everyone
  • oting: (noun) everything
  • olu: (noun) everywhere
  • otai: (adv) always, every time
  • dapwalu: (noun) forest
  • dapwa: (noun) tree
  • pwaping: (noun) leaf
  • wang: (adj) yellow
  • layang: (adj) red (hot-color)
  • wanglayang: (compound) orange (yellow-red)
  • sweyang: (compound) brown (dirt-color)

Grammar: The Universal Quantifier "o"

The quantifier o means "all" or "every". It works just like other quantifiers (i, a, na) and comes before the noun.

  • o mau du ma kagu. - All cats like food.
  • mi du si o ting. - I see everything.
  • o mang du bi ilu. - Everyone is here.

Story: o pwaping du su

itai du bi sukwatai (autumn).

ne dapwalu, o pwaping de o dapwa du su.

omang du kumeng i. o tai, o daswetai (year), i du swadu (happen).

na pwaping so su du yo o yang. na pwaping so layang, so wanglayang, so sweyang.

o wimang du ma du go olu ne dapwalu.

ina du si o ting so ga. ina so gali.

(Now it is autumn.)

(In the forest, all the leaves of all the trees are falling.)

(Everyone knows this. Every time, every year, this happens.)

(The fallen leaves have all the colors. Leaves are red, orange, and brown.)

(All the children love to go everywhere in the forest.)

(They see all the good things. They are happy.)

Sesowi Lesson 14: Habitual Actions

Vocabulary

  • natai: (adv) usually, customarily, many times (number-time)
  • pumenglu: (noun) school (learn-place)
  • badu: (noun) project (with-do)
  • bamang: (noun) partner, companion (with-person)
  • batai: (noun) class, meeting (with-time)
  • gasi: (adj) pretty, beautiful (good-see)
  • gi: (noun) hill, height
  • dagi: (noun) mountain (big-hill)
  • sugi: (adj) deep (down-hill)
  • lolu: (noun) lake (water-place)
  • dalolu: (noun) ocean (big-lake)

Grammar: The Habitual Tense with "so natai"

To express that an action happens regularly or is a habit (e.g., "I walk to school" vs. "I am walking to school"), you can use the adverbial phrase so natai after the verb.

  • Present Continuous: mi du kogo tu pumenglu. - I am walking to school.
  • Present Habitual: mi du kogo so natai tu pumenglu. - I [usually] walk to school.
  • Past Habitual: mi ya du kogo so natai tu pumenglu. - I used to walk to school.

Story: mi ya du sa ilu

mi ya du sa ilu so lang tai.

ya, mi ya du kogo so natai tu dagi. mi ya du ma du si dalolu de ta dagi.

itai, mi no mai du kogo so natai tu dagi. goko de mi so noga.

geng, mi du kogo so natai tu lolu so winai. i lolu so sugi so gasi.

o kiboi so kogo ba mi so natai.

mi du meng i du bi so ga.

(I have lived here for a long time.)

(In the past, I used to walk to the mountain. I loved to see the ocean from the top of the mountain.)

(Now, I no longer walk to the mountain. My legs are bad.)

(But, I usually walk to the nearby lake. The lake is deep and pretty.)

(All the birds usually walk with me.)

(I think it is good.)

Sesowi Lesson 15: It Is Good To...

Vocabulary

  • kogo: (verb) to walk
  • logo: (verb) to swim
  • gasang: (verb) to sing
  • pumeng: (verb) to study
  • pe: (noun/verb) play, fun; to play
  • nogwa: (adj) difficult, hard (no-easy)
  • gwa: (adj) easy, smooth
  • nole: (adj) unofficial, illegal
  • le: (adj) official, legal

Grammar: Verbs as Subjects (Gerunds/Infinitives)

In English, we say "To walk is good" or "Walking is good." The action itself is the subject of the sentence. In Sesowi, you do this by simply putting the verb phrase (with du) at the beginning of the sentence and treating it like a noun.

  • du kogo so ga. - Walking is good. / It is good to walk.
  • du pumeng sesowi so nogwa. - Learning Sesowi is difficult.
  • du bi pumengmang so pe. - Being a student is fun.

Story: du kogo so ga

mamang de mi du ese, "e ting so ga du du?"

mi du se, "du kogo so ga. du kogo so natai so mai ga."

i du se, "geng du kogo ne dapwalu so noga."

mi du se, "dwe. du kogo ne dapwalu po so nogwa. geng du si o boi so pe."

du pe ba daga so ga. du sangsi kiboi du gasang so ga. du pumeng se so nu so ga.

du bi so ga so da gali!

(My friend asks, "What is a good thing to do?")

(I say, "Walking is good. Walking usually is even better.")

(He says, "But walking in the forest is bad.")

(I say, "True. Walking in the forest can be difficult. But seeing all the animals is fun.")

("Playing with a dog is good. Listening to birds sing is good. Learning a new language is good.")

("Being good is very happy!")

Sesowi Lesson 16: "There is" and The Passive

Vocabulary

  • yo: (verb) to have; (impersonal) there is, there are
  • sa: (verb) life, experience; (passive marker) to experience something
  • dabauboi: (noun) pet (house-animal)
  • gekanglu: (noun) store, market (pay-place)
  • swego: (verb) to dig
  • twating: (noun) hole
  • nogapu: (verb) to steal (bad-get)
  • yokang: (adj) rich (have-money)
  • kang: (noun) metal, money

Grammar Points

  1. "There is..." with du yo: To say "there is" or "there are," you can start a sentence with du yo.
    • du yo dana mang ne gekanglu. - There are many people in the store.
    • du yo wa mau ta dabau. - There is a cat on the house.
  2. The Experiential Passive with sa: To say something "was done" to a subject, you can use the verb sa (to experience). This is one way to form a passive voice.
    • Active: daga ya du ka kagu. - The dog ate the food.
    • Passive: kagu ya du sa ka. - The food was eaten (lit. 'experienced eating').

Story: kang so du sa nogapu

du yo mang so yokang. i ya du lu o kang de i ne dabau.

wa nitai, i ya du pingtai. kang de i ya du sa nogapu.

bebaitai, i ya so nogali. i ya du se, "o kang de mi ya du go nosi!"

mamang de i ya du ese, "e tai i ya du sa nogapu?"

i ya du se, "mi no du kumeng. mi ya du pingtai."

du yo twating ne kusai (wall) de dabau. po, mang so noga ya du dai i du go ne.

(There was a rich person. He put all his money in his house.)

(One night, he was sleeping. His money was stolen.)

(The next day, he was sad. He said, "All my money has disappeared!")

(His friend asked, "When was it stolen?")

(He said, "I don't know. I was sleeping.")

(There was a hole in the wall of the house. Maybe a bad person used it to go inside.)

Sesowi Lesson 17: "Just" and "Already"

Vocabulary

  • swa: (adv/pronoun) self; just (modifies tense)
  • ya: (adv) already (in addition to past tense)
  • igo: (verb) to come (this-go)
  • ogo: (verb) to arrive (all-go)
  • kigo: (noun) flight
  • kigolu: (noun) airport (flight-place)
  • nonutai: (adj) late (not-new-time)
  • nutai: (adj) early (new-time)
  • lubi: (verb) to wait (place-be)
  • omeng: (verb) to decide

Grammar: Time Nuances

  1. "Just now" with swa: Placing swa before a tense marker adds a sense of immediacy.
    • mi swa ya du ogo. - I just arrived.
    • mi swa be du go. - I am just about to go.
  2. "Already" with ya: When not part of a past-tense construction, ya can mean "already". In a past-tense sentence, it emphasizes completion before another past event (pluperfect).
    • mi ya du kumeng. - I already know.
    • tai yu ya du ogo, mi ya ya du ka. - When you arrived, I had already eaten.

Story: kigo so nonutai

mi dwa ya du bi lu kigolu. kigo de mi be du go.

geng mi ya du ogo so nonutai! mi ya so nogali.

mamang de mi, so ya ya du ogo, ya du ese mi, "kigo ya ya du go e?"

mi du se, "mi no du kumeng!"

mi ya du go tu lemang (officer). "kigo de mi swa ya du go e?"

i lemang ya du se, "no. i swa be du go. yu so ga!"

mi ya so da gali! mi swa be du go!

(I had to be at the airport. My flight was going to leave.)

(But I arrived late! I was sad.)

(My friend, who had already arrived, asked me, "Had the flight already left?")

(I said, "I don't know!")

(I went to an officer. "Did my flight just leave?")

(The officer said, "No. It is just about to leave. You are good!")

(I was very happy! I was just about to go!)

Sesowi Lesson 18: If and When (Conditionals)

Vocabulary

  • bi: (conjunction) if
  • sulo: (noun) rain
  • takulo: (noun) snow (over-ice)
  • nola: (adj) cold
  • la: (adj) hot
  • mela: (adj) warm (middle-hot)
  • baitaili: (noun) weather (day-energy)
  • tabai: (noun) sun (over-light)
  • nitabai: (noun) moon (dark-sun)

Grammar: Conditional Sentences with "bi"

To create a conditional (if-then) sentence, you start the "if" clause with bi. The "then" is usually implied by the next clause. Sesowi does not distinguish between indicative ("if it rains") and subjunctive ("if it were to rain"). Context makes the meaning clear.

  • bi i be du sulo, mi no be du go. - If it rains, I will not go.
  • bi yu ya so gali, mi ya so gali. - If you were happy, I was happy.
  • bi mi du yo kang, mi be du pu dabau so da. - If I had money, I would get a big house.

Story: bi baitaili so ga

bebaitai, mina be du yo pe ne dapwalu.

geng, mina wating nang du go bi baitaili so ga.

bi i be du sulo, mina be du sa ne dabau.

bi i be du takulo, mina be du loka lo so la.

bi tabai be du bai, mina be du kogo.

bi mi du bi yu, mi be du yau tabai. mi no du ma sulo.

geng bi i du sulo, du pe ne dapwalu laiting so pe!

(Tomorrow, we will have a party in the forest.)

(But, we can only go if the weather is good.)

(If it rains, we will stay in the house.)

(If it snows, we will drink hot water (tea/cocoa).)

(If the sun shines, we will walk.)

(If I were you, I would want sun. I don't like rain.)

(But if it rains, playing in the forest is also fun!)

Sesowi Lesson 19: Making a Choice (Whether)

Vocabulary

  • ese: (verb) to ask (what-talk)
  • laise: (verb) to answer (other-talk)
  • loboi: (noun) fish (water-animal)
  • kubauboi: (noun) turtle, crustacean (shell-animal)
  • dalolu: (noun) ocean
  • lolu: (noun) lake
  • langlo: (noun) river (long-water)
  • gese: (verb) to claim, propose
  • dwemeng: (verb) to believe, trust (truth-think)
  • pomeng: (verb) to suspect (possibility-think)

Grammar: "Whether" with an embedded question

In English, we say "I don't know whether he is coming." The "whether" part is a yes/no question embedded in the sentence. In Sesowi, you do this literally by putting the yes/no question (with e at the end) as the object of the main verb.

  • mi no du kumeng i be du igo e. - I don't know whether he will come.
  • yu dwa du ese i du bi mamang de yu e. - You should ask whether she is your friend.
  • i ya du ese mi du ma loboi e. - He asked whether I like fish.

Story: e lu du go?

mamang de mi ya du ese mi, "mina be du go e lu bebaitai?"

mi ya du laise, "mi no du kumeng. mi pomeng mina nang du go tu dalolu."

i ya du ese mi, "yu du kumeng bi du yo na loboi alu e?"

mi ya du se, "mi no du kumeng. mi dwa du bu i."

betai, mi ya du laise, "dwe. du yo na loboi. du yo na kubauboi laiting."

i ya du se, "ga! mi no du ma kubauboi."

pauting, mina ya du omeng du go tu langlo.

(My friend asked me, "Where will we go tomorrow?")

(I answered, "I don't know. I suspect we can go to the ocean.")

(She asked me, "Do you know whether there are fish there?")

(I said, "I don't know. I have to look for it.")

(Later, I answered, "Yes. There are many fish. There are also many turtles.")

(She said, "Good! I don't like turtles.")

(Finally, we decided to go to the river.)

Sesowi Lesson 20: Review and A Full Story

Vocabulary

This lesson uses vocabulary from all previous lessons. Here are a few important compounds to review.

  • badu bamang: project partners
  • laiting: also, or
  • yabaitai: yesterday
  • bebaitai: tomorrow
  • otai: always
  • natai: usually
  • swatai: just now
  • omeng: to decide
  • kumeng: to know
  • dwemeng: to believe
  • pomeng: to suspect

Grammar Review

This lesson's story combines all the grammar points we have learned:

  • Basic Sentences: S + du + V + O
  • Adjectives & Adverbs: Using so
  • Tense & Modals: ya, be, nang, dwa, po
  • Possession & Prepositions: de, tu, ne, ba
  • Questions & Commands: e? and ba!
  • Comparatives: mai, gengmai
  • Relative Clauses: Noun so [Sentence]
  • Conditionals: bi ...

Story: omeng so da (A Big Decision)

odisa (Odis) du bi mau so du yo dana omeng so dawe.

o nutai (morning), i du tau (jump) tu ta yotingpingting (table).

ta yotingpingting du yo do lobau (cup) de lo. o do du bi lo tu mau.

odisa du ogo tu me i do bau. i du si tu wa bau.

betai, i du si tu lai bau.

i du meng so da ku, po dono wiwitai (second).

so pau, i du omeng. i kai (start) du loka de wa lobau.

wina wiwitai be, i du nogo, du laitu (turn), du loka de lai lobau.

mi du emeng (wonder) so da, i mau du meng e ting. i du omeng e we?

i du kumeng so i du loka o lo e? dwe. i du kumeng. i nomai du yau du pe.

i pe so mai dawe tu i tu loka.

(Odis is a cat who has many important decisions.)

(Every morning, he jumps onto the table.)

(On the table there are two cups of water. Both are water for the cat.)

(Odis arrives between these two cups. He looks at one cup.)

(Then, he looks at the other cup.)

(He thinks very hard, maybe for 20 seconds.)

(In the end, he decides. He starts to drink from one cup.)

(A few seconds later, he stops, turns, and drinks from the other cup.)

(I really wonder, what is this cat thinking? How does he decide?)

(Does he know that he will drink all the water? Yes. He knows. He just wants to play.)

(The game is more important to him than drinking.)
