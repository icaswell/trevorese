Grammar 4: Focus on various more specific things that crop up in English
“Whether”

“Whether” is a word in English that introduces a clause representing a yes-no question. Sesowi, like Chinese, does this more simply by using a question particle at the end of a statement.

en gloss
Do you know whether that’s a dog? you do know that be dog what?
He asked whether it was a dog. he back do ask that be dog what?
Animacy

Many languages treat nouns differently depending on whether they are animate (e.g., animals) or inanimate (e.g., rocks), and may even have different articles or pronouns. English makes an animate/inanimate distinction for some words, e.g., who vs. what, someone vs. something. While adjectives like "young" typically describe animate beings and "new" describes inanimate objects, there can be overlap. Sesowi does not grammatically distinguish animacy, but if clarity is needed, “thing” or “person”/“being” can be added:

en gloss
what/whom do you see? you do see what?
what do you see? (inanimate) you do see what thing?
whom do you see? (animate) you do see what person?
a young person wears a new hat person like new do wear hat like new
“It” as “that”

A special case of impersonal pronouns are expressions like “It is a pig!”, where “it” functions like “this” or “that”. In English, this is more or less the same as saying, “This is a pig!”. Sesowi uses the equivalent of "this" or "that" in these contexts.

en gloss
What’s that? It’s a pig! <What is that? That’s a pig!>
that be what? that be pig!
Who’s at the door? It’s your mom! <Who is at the door? This is your mom.>
what person be at door? this be mother of you!
It’s a boy! this be male-child!
Impersonal “It”/“You”/“They”

English often uses pronouns like "it," "you," or "they" in an impersonal sense to refer to people in general. For example:

  • It is generally believed that X...
  • You never know what’s going to happen.
  • You know what they say.
  • People are worried. (Or: They are worried.)

In many cases, these can be translated into Sesowi using a general word like "people," or the intended meaning can be inferred from context. For instance, some languages like Hebrew use a third-person plural ("they") with pro-drop (omitting the pronoun) to convey this impersonal meaning.

Yes and No

Sesowi has one word for “yes” and one word for “no”:

en gloss
Are you happy? you be happy what?
yes. truth
no. untruth

These can also function as “correct” and “incorrect”:

The existence of dedicated words for "yes" and "no" may seem straightforward to English speakers, but it's not a universal feature in language. Latin, Irish, and Chinese, for example, traditionally answer yes-no questions by repeating the verb. This is why Hiberno-English (Irish English) features exchanges like:

  • Do you want tea?
    • I do. (Instead of “Yes.”)

Some languages have more than two options. French uses “si” to contradict a negative statement, and German uses “doch” for the same purpose. Early Modern English actually had a four-form system: yes, no, yea, and nay. "Yea" and "nay" answered positive questions, while "yes" and "no" were used to answer negative questions.

In Sesowi, to emphasize a contradiction, you can add “but”:

en gloss
Are you not studying? you untruth do study what?
(Yes, I am studying.) but truth! me do study.
Verbs with Implied Motion

In languages like English, many verbs imply directionality, like come/go and bring/take. In Sesowi, there is often one neutral verb for the action, and if direction is needed, it is specified with words like this-way (towards the speaker) and that-way (away from the speaker).

en gloss
she came to my house she back do move this-way to house of me
she went to the store she back do move that-way to store
she brought an apple to the store (with her) she back do carry apple move that-way to store
she took an apple from the store she back do carry apple from store
Verbing Nouns

In English, there are various ways to turn a noun into an action (e.g., you play a violin, you do pushups, you sing a song). In Sesowi, you can often use the noun directly as a verb with the do particle to indicate it is an action.

en gloss
She plays piano. she do piano
She does pushups. she do pushup
“Too” vs. “Very”

Several languages, like Hindi and Chinese, don’t have a separate word for “too” (excessively) and “very” (to a high degree). Sesowi distinguishes them. “Too” can be expressed with a word like over-much.

Pronominal “One” and “Some”

In English, we can use “one” to substitute for a singular noun and “some” for a plural noun that has already been mentioned. In Sesowi, the words for one and number (or a plural marker) can be used similarly.

en gloss
I saw a bird. Did you see one? me back do see bird. you back do see one what?
I saw some birds. Did you see some? me back do see bird-plural. you back do see number what?
“Alone” and “Only”

In Sesowi, the concepts of "alone" (by oneself) and "only" (exclusively) can be expressed using adverbs derived from one.

en gloss
He was sitting alone. he back do sit like-one-person
He was only sitting. (He was doing nothing else) he back do sit one-action
I only talked to you. (I talked to no one else) me do talk to you one-person
I only want one thing. me do want one thing only
Beyond “This” and “That”

When differentiating between two subjects, English uses “this” and “that”.

en gloss
Which cat are you talking about? This one or that one? you do talk about what cat? this or that?

However, if you have more than two subjects, this gets confusing in English, and we often resort to emphasis or descriptions like "the former" and "the latter". In Sesowi, you can simply say “that-two”, “that-three”, and so on to distinguish multiple entities.

en gloss
I see this cat, that cat, and that other cat. me do see this cat, that cat, and that-two cat
This cat meowed at that cat, and then the third cat meowed back. this cat back do cat-sound to that cat, then that-two cat back do cat-sound
