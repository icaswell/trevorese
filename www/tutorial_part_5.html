Grammar 4: focus on various more specific things that crop up in English
“Whether”
“Whether” is a funny word in English. Basically, it just introduces a clause that is a yes-no question. Trevorese, like Chinese, does this much more simply.
en gloss
Do you know whether that’s a dog? you do know this be dog what?
He asked whether it was a dog this back do ask this be dog what?
Animacy
Many languages treat nouns differently depending on whether they are animate (e.g. animals) or not (e.g. rocks), and may even have different articles. English uses the animate/inanimate distinction for some words, e.g. who vs what, him vs it, someone vs. something, young vs new. Trevorese does not make this distinction in any cases, but if clarity is needed, “thing” or “being” can be added in:
en gloss
what/whom do you see? you do see what?
what do you see? you do see what thing?
whom do you see? you do see what person?
a young person wears a new hat person new DO wear hat new
“It” as “that”
A special case of the impersonal are expressions like “it is a pig!”, when “it” basically means the same thing as “that” or “this” anyways: even in English, this is more or less the same as saying “This is a pig!”.
en gloss
What’s that? It’s a pig! <What is that? That’s a pig!> that do be what? That do be pig!
Who’s at the door? It’s your mom! <What is at door? This is your mom> what person do be at door? this do be mom of you!
It’s a boy! this (do be) boy!
“It”/“you”/“They”: “People”
They say that this “passive”/“impersonal” phenomenon is a deep conspiracy that cuts across all pronouns. It’s everywhere. You can’t get away from it. Whether one recognizes it or not, the spectrum of impersonal phenomena extends deep into the language…or so it is said. In reality, you can just translate these however you want. Just listing them here for completeness:
  • It is generally believed that X
  • you never know what’s going to happen
  • You know what they say
  • People are worried
Hebrew: “they say” but with pro-drop. Prodrop implies third if no context.
Yes and no
Trevorese has one word for yes and one word for no:
en gloss
Are you happy? you like happy what?
yes. truth
no. no
These are the same as the words for “correct” and “incorrect”:
en gloss
Are you happy? you like happy what?
correct. truth
incorrect. no
English speakers or Spanish speakers may be confused as to why this gets its own section. How complex can “yes” and “no” be? However, “yes” and “no” are by no means a common phenomenon in language. Latin, Irish, Chinese, Toki Pona, and many others have no word for yes or no: they just repeat the verb. Hence why you see in Hiberno English, whose substrate is irish, exchanges like the following
  • do you want tea?
    • I do. (Not “yes”)
There are also many languages with more than two words for yes or no. French uses the word si when saying yes and contradicting something. German uses the word doch to contradict the thing that was just said, so it can mean either yes or no depending on the context. Older English had four words: TODO. Child English also has four: yes, no, yuh-huh, nuh-huh. (Note that these are different from “m-hm” and “m-m”, which are forms of “u-huh” and “uh-uh”, which simply mean “yes” and “no”.) In Trevorese, if you want to emphasize that you are contradicting a statement, you can add “but” before it:
en gloss
You are studying? you do study what?
yes. truth
You are studying Chinese, right? you do study Chinese what?
but no! I’m studying Trevorese. against no! i do study Trevorese.
Verbs with implied motion
In many languages, like English, many verbs are identical save for the directionality, like come/go, bring/take. In Trevorese, there is only one verb for each, and if context is needed, thatway and thisway are added.
en gloss
she came to my house this back do go big-container of me
she went to the store this back do go [that way] store
she came to the store this back do go [this way] store
she carried an apple this back do carry apple
she brought an apple to the store this back do carry apple that way to store
she took an apple from the store this back do carry apple that way from store
verbing nouns
In English, there are a variety of bespoke verbs to verbify different nouns. For instance, you play a violin, you do pushups, you sing a song. In Trevorese, these are all omitted. You just use the do particle to indicate that it is a verb:
en gloss
She plays piano this do piano
She does pushups this do number pushup
To have, to want, to try
These are verbs that are absent in many languages. Trevorese has them.
too vs very contrast
Several languages, like Hindi and Chinese, don’t differentiate “too” and “very”. This seems like an odd failing. Trevorese distinguishes them. “Too” is “overmore”
“one” and “some”
In English, we can use “one” to refer to something that has already been mentioned in conversation, or “some” to refer to a plural thing that has already been mentioned in conversation. In Trevorese, this is identical: one and number are used:
en gloss
I saw a bird. Did you see one? me back do see air-animal. you back do see one what?
I saw one me back do see one
I saw some me back do see pl

Beyond this and that
Sometimes one needs to refer to two different subjects, and to differentiate them, you need to use “this” and “that”:
en gloss
which cat are you talking about? This one or that one? you do talk what cat? this other-thing that?
However, if you have more than two subjects, this gets rather confusing to deal with in English, and we usually say use emphasis. In Trevorese you can simply say “that-two”, “that-three”, and so on:
en gloss
I see this cat, that cat, the other cat, and the other other cat me do see this cat, that cat, that-two cat, that three cat
this cat meowed at that cat, and then THAT cat meowed at that cat this cat back do cat-sound that cat, and that time that two cat back do cat-sound that cat
