Grammar 1: the basics
The following introduces English,Gloss, and Trevorese forms. The gloss is just Trevorese using (approximate) English words, so it is easier to see exactly what’s going on. There may also be some loose literal translations in < angle brackets >. These are loose translations of the Trevorese into English so you can understand more or less how the construction words. For instance:
  • English: I saw the black cat
  • Loose translation: <I did see the cat like blackness >
  • gloss: me back do see cat like dark-color
  • Trevorese: mi ya du si mau se nobailang
Nouns
There is no distinction between a and the. Therefore:
en gloss
cat cat
a cat cat
the cat cat
If you want to specify a particular one, you can use quantifiers like this, what, and what:
en gloss
this cat this cat
that cat that cat
what cat? what cat?
Now let’s introduce a simple verb: be. For any verb in the present tense, you need to use the word do to mark that it is a verb:
en gloss
this cat is a friend this cat do be love-person
a dog is an animal dog do be person
To negate this, just add no right before the verb:
en gloss
that cat is not a friend that cat no do be love-person
Plurals
You may notice that in the previous examples, the word for cats was the same as the word for cat. In Trevorese, the plural looks the same as the singular! If you speak a European language, you may think this will cause ambiguity. However, usually the quantifier makes it unambiguous as to whether the noun is plural or not. For instance, with a quantifier like many or sixty-seven, you already know that the noun following it is plural. This said, there are a few examples of when context alone is not enough to determine whether something is plural. For example, consider the sentence I was trying to sleep when I heard some cat meow vs I was trying to sleep when I heard some cats meow; or this cat is chonky vs these cats are chonky. In these cases, you can use the word number to mark the plural:
en gloss
A dog dog
dogs [two or more] number dog
that dog is a friend that dog do be love-person
those dogs are friends that number dog do be love-person
Literally, this just means “group”. It goes before the noun, because it is a quantifier, just like this or that.
Adjectives 1
Let’s look at some modifiers, aka adjectives/adverbs. In Trevorese, there is no difference between nouns and adjectives! You just say that a noun is “like'” another noun with like:
en gloss
the cat cat
the black cat cat like dark-color
the cat is black cat like dark-color
the cat is talkative cat like talk
this cat is friendly this cat like love-being
that cat is not friendly this cat no like love-being
a friend is friendly love-being like love-being
what dog is cat-like? what dog like cat?
those dogs friendly that number dog like love-being
Another way of thinking about like is that’s: “the black cat” is expressed as <the cat that’s black>. With multiple adjectives, put a like before each one:
en gloss
this cat is friendly, talkative, and doglike this cat like love-person like talk, like dog
Verbs 1
We already saw the verb do be. Let’s talk more about verbs. There are not truly verbs in this language; you can just “do” a noun. Just like in English, you can substitute I squat with I do a squat, or I boop with I do a boop:
en gloss
the cat is talking cat do talk
the cat is not talking cat no do talk
While we’re at it, let’s learn the tenses. In Trevorese, there are three basic tenses: past, present, and future. They are expressed with back, do, and front:
en gloss
the cat talked cat back do talk
the cat is talking / the cat talks cat do talk
the cat will talk cat front do talk
Past, present, future – that’s it! Now that we can form a full sentence, let’s go back to adjectives. Adjectives go after the thing they modify:
en gloss
the friendly cat is talking cat like good-person do talk
the cat is talking friendlily/in a friendly way cat do talk like good-person
that cat is talking unfriendlily cat do talk like no good-person
the black dog is talking in a friendly, cat-like way dog like dark-color do talk like good-person like cat
he sings sweetly, like a small bird this do good-sound like sugar, like air-animal like small
he spoke to me like a child this back do talk me like small-person
Notes: the penultimate sentence is somewhat ambiguous in Trevorese, but contextually it’s clear. the last sentence is ambiguous in English and Trevorese both.
Pronouns
At this point we can talk about pronouns:
en gloss
I / me me
you you
he / she / him / her / it this
The plural forms of these are formed with number, just like the plural of a noun:
en gloss
we / us number me
you all number you
they / them number this
Here are examples using them in sentences:
en gloss
I am a cat me do be cat
I am black me like dark-color
I am talking me do talk
You may notice that this means both he / she, as well as “this”! Therefore, the following are all valid translations, and need context to tell apart:
en gloss
he is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
she is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
this is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
Similarly, these two sentences are the same in Trevorese:
en gloss
this cat is black this cat like dark-color
this black cat this cat like dark-color
“Thing”
Any verb, noun, or adjective can be replaced with “thing” if the context is clear. For instance:
  • We were thinging football
  • I didn’t hear the thing you just thinged
Prepositions
Prepositions are words that say how two things are related. For instance, you can say that the dog is behind the cat, over the cat, in the cat, for the cat; or that it is woofing at the cat, or with the cat, or by the cat. There are no true prepositions in Trevorese – everything is ultimately a noun. When you would use a preposition to describe where something is in English, just use the noun for corresponding location in Trevorese. If a dog is on the roof, you say that the dog is surface the roof. If you are going to the store, you are going direction the store; and if you are going from the store, you are going of the store. If the dog jumps onto the roof, it jumps directionsurface the roof. The following sections give details about how common prepositions are used.

Possession with “of”

Let’s start with of, aka the word meaning belonging/possession. This word indicates possession:
en gloss
This is my dog this do be dog of me
This is my dog’s toy this do be play-thing of dog of me
The independent forms mine/yours/ours/hers/its/his/theirs are handled simply by dropping the subject, as in Chinese:
en gloss
This is mine this do be of me
This is my dog. Where is yours? this dog do be of me. of you what place?
of can also mean “from”, as we will see in the next section.

Coexistence with “with”

The word with is very straightforward. It is used just as with or and in English:
en gloss
The person is with the cat person do be with cat
Come with me! you do go with me please!
I see a dog and a cat me do see dog with cat
he mixed water with soil this back do with-do water with dirt
he mixed water with a stick this back do use stick do with-do water
Note that in the last example, the English “with” here is an instrumental marker in English, and actually a quite different meaning from the normal meaning. For the normal meaning of “with”, this would mean “He and a shovel were digging a hole”!

Basic Prepositions of movement

en gloss
She drove to the store this back do drive direction store
She drove from the store this back do drive of store
She drove through the store this back do drive through store
She was driving at the store this back do drive place store

Spatial prepositions

English has a lot of prepositions for how two objects can be related. The snake can be in front of the dog, near the dog, inside the dog, above the dog, on the dog, or by the side of the dog. Some of these change if there is movement: for instance, after the snake goes into the dog it is inside the dog. In Trevorese, these prepositions are simply expressed as one of the basic prepositions of movement (usually direction) plus the location relative to the object. Let’s look at a few examples. In these examples, we’ll break them up by whether the movement is static (happening in one location), or as a movement to- or from that location: location noun: inside
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming inside the dog long-animal do water-move inside dog static
the snake is swimming into the dog long-animal do water-move direction inside dog movement
the snake is swimming out from the dog long-animal do water-move of inside dog rev. mvmnt.
location noun: surface
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming on the dog long-animal do water-move surface dog static
the snake is swimming onto the dog long-animal do water-move direction surface dog movement
the snake is swimming out from on top of the dog long-animal do water-move of surface dog rev. mvmnt.
location noun: over
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming over the dog long-animal do water-move over dog static
the snake is swimming to the area over the dog long-animal do water-move direction over dog movement
the snake is swimming out from the area over the dog long-animal do water-move of over dog rev. mvmnt.
the snake is swimming over the dog long-animal do water-move through over dog through
location noun: small-distance
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming near the dog long-animal do water-move small-distance dog static
the snake is swimming near to where the dog is long-animal do water-move direction small-distance dog movement
the snake is swimming out from near where the dog is long-animal do water-move of small-distance dog rev. mvmnt.
the snake is swimming past the dog long-animal do water-move through small-distance dog through

Temporal prepositions

Prepositions of time are expressed with the same words as those of space. Imagine yourself as a box where the future stretches out in front of you and the past behind. Now imagine that you yourself are an event in time. You see another event in the future, in front of you. This event happens after you in time — so in this spatial metaphor, it happens in front of you. You happen before it — so, spatially, you happen behind it. Say there is another event that starts before you and ends after you. You happen during that event — spatially, you happen in it. And if you (the event) started at the same time as the other event and continue on forwards in time — aka you happen since that event — spatially, you happen starting at that event.1

Prepositions in verb phrases

Many Indo-European verbs have prepositions built into them, and it causes considerable chaos for learners to master them. In Trevorese, these prepositions are generally absent:
en gloss
I worked on a project me back do hard-do project
What are you talking about? you do talk what?
Which person are you talking about? you do talk what person?
he depends on me this do depend me
She believes in God this do truth-think God
Look at this! you do see this please
what are you looking for? you do seek what?
They listen to music this number do sound-see good-sound
I am coping with loss. me do cope loss
An exception is verbs that take indirect objects with the preposition to, like talk to. These are explained in the section on indirect objects. In some cases where English has different meanings for different prepositions, Trevorese has different verbs. In these cases you’ll notice that English also generally offers us easy synonyms:
en gloss
What are you looking at? you do see what-thing?
What are you looking for? you do seek what-thing?
What are youlooking up? you do seek what-thing?
What are youlooking after? you do care what-thing?
In other cases there is some ambiguity:
en gloss
She believes me this do truth-think me
She believes in God this do truth-think God
She believes in me this do trust me
Notice however that even in English, believe in has a different meaning when applied to God versus to me! Really, there is ambiguity everywhere in all languages, but context clears almost everything up!
