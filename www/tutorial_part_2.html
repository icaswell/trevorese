Grammar 1: the basics
The following introduces English, Gloss, and Sesowi forms. The gloss for a word is just an English word that approximates the Sesowi word's meaning, so it is easier to see exactly what’s going on. There may also be some loose literal translations in < angle brackets >. These are loose translations of the Sesowi into English so you can understand more or less how the construction words. For instance:
  • English: I saw the black cat
  • Loose translation: <I did see the cat like blackness >
  • gloss: me back do see cat like dark-color
  • Sesowi: mi ya du si mau se nobailang
Nouns
There is no distinction between a and the. Therefore:
en gloss
cat cat
a cat cat
the cat cat
If you want to specify a particular one, you can use quantifiers like this (this), that (that), and what:
en gloss
this cat this cat
that cat that cat
what cat? what cat?
Now let’s introduce a simple verb: be. For any verb in the present tense, you need to use the word do to mark that it is a verb:
en gloss
this cat is a friend this cat do be love-person
a dog is an animal big-good do be animal
To negate this, just add no right before the verb:
en gloss
that cat is not a friend that cat no do be love-person
a dog is not an animal big-good no do be animal
Plurals
In Sesowi, the plural looks the same as the singular! Let's look at the examples we just saw, and show what alternate English translations could be:
en gloss
a dog is an animal big-good do be animal
dogs are animals big-good do be animal
If you speak a European language, you may think this will cause ambiguity. However, many of the world's languages, Like Indonesian, Chinese, Vietnamese, Balinese, etc. get along fine without separate plural forms! If there is any ambiguity you can use a quantifier to be extra clear. We have already seen the quantifiers this (this) and that (that), which are singular by default. Let's introduce two more quantifiers: number (plural/group) to explicitly mark the plural, and one (one), which is literally just the number one, and can be used to unambiguously mark the singular. Let's see some examples:
en gloss
a dog / dogs (ambiguous) big-good
one dog (unambiguous singular) one big-good
this/that dog (unambiguous singular) this/that big-good
dogs (unambiguous plural) number big-good
that dog is a friend (unambiguous singular) that big-good do be love-person
those dogs are friends that number big-good do be love-person
Linguist's corner: Many languages, e.g. Hindi, lack an indefinite article like "a" use "one" as the singularizer.
Adjectives 1
Let’s look at some modifiers, aka adjectives/adverbs. In Sesowi, there is no difference between nouns and adjectives! You just say that a noun is “like” another noun with like. Another way of thinking about like is that it means who’s or that’s. Let's look at a few nouns:
en gloss
a cat cat
blackness dark-color
a friend love-person
speech/talk talk
Now let's turn them into adjectives with like :
en loose gloss
a cat < cat > cat
a black cat < cat [who's] like blackness > cat like dark-color
a talkative cat < cat [who's] like talk > cat like talk
a friendly cat < cat [who's] like friend> cat like love-person
a cat-like friend < friend [who's] like cat > love-person like cat
More examples:
en gloss
the cat cat
the black cat cat like dark-color
the cat is black cat like dark-color
the cat is talkative cat like talk
this cat is friendly this cat like love-person
that cat is not friendly this cat no like love-person
a friend is friendly love-person like love-person
what dog is cat-like? what big-good like cat?
those dogs friendly that number big-good like love-person

With multiple adjectives, put a like before each one:
en gloss
this cat is friendly, talkative, and doglike this cat like love-person like talk, like big-good
Verbs 1
We already saw the verb be. Let’s talk more about verbs. There are not truly verbs in this language; you can just “do” a noun. Just like in English, you can substitute I squat with I do a squat, or I boop with I do a boop:
en loose gloss
the cat is talking < the cat is doin' a say > cat do talk
the cat is not talking < the cat is not doin' a say > cat no do talk
While we’re at it, let’s learn the tenses. You can express past/future by imagining that you are looking out and seeing the future actions in front of you and the past actions behind you, and use back (back/behind), and front (front):
en gloss
the cat is talking / the cat talks cat do talk
the cat talked cat back do talk
the cat will talk cat front do talk
Past, present, future – that’s it! Now that we can form a full sentence, let’s look at how we can modify verbs. Adverbs are formed the exact some way as we just saw with adjectives, using like after the verb they modify. Let's practice adverbs + adjectives:
en gloss
the friendly cat is talking cat like love-person do talk
the cat is talking friendlily/in a friendly way cat do talk like love-person
that cat is talking unfriendlily cat do talk like no love-person
the black dog is talking in a friendly, cat-like way big-good like dark-color do talk like love-person like cat
he sings sweetly, like a small bird this do good-sound like sugar, like air-animal like small
Pronouns
At this point we can talk about pronouns:
en gloss
I / me me
you you
he / she / him / her / it this
The plural forms of these are formed with number:
en gloss
we / us me-number
you all you-number
they / them this-number
Here are examples using them in sentences:
en gloss
I am a cat me do be cat
I am black me like dark-color
I am talking me do talk
You may notice that this means both he / she, as well as “this”! Therefore, the following are all valid translations, and need context to tell apart:
en gloss
he is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
she is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
this is a black cat this do be cat like dark-color
Note: this is the same as in e.g. Hindi, where "woh" means "he/she/that".
Similarly, these two sentences are the same in Sesowi:
en gloss
this cat is black this cat like dark-color
this black cat this cat like dark-color
Prepositions
Prepositions are words that say how two things are related. For instance, you can say that the dog is behind the cat, over the cat, in the cat, for the cat; or that it is woofing at the cat, or with the cat, or by the cat.

Sesowi has three "true" prepositions: of (of/from), to (to), and with (with). All location or movement prepositions are expressed with nouns saying what the location/direction is. The following sections give details about how common prepositions are used, starting with the "true" prepositions, and then going into the prepositions of movement/location.

Possession with of

Let’s start with of, aka the word meaning belonging/possession. This word indicates possession:
en gloss
This is my dog this do be big-good of me
This is my dog’s toy this do be play-thing of big-good of me
The independent forms mine/yours/ours/hers/its/his/theirs are handled simply by dropping the subject, as in Chinese:
en gloss
This is mine this do be of me
This is my dog. Where is yours? this big-good do be of me. of you what place?
This word can also mean "about" in the sense of talking about something, just as in English we can say "talk of":
en gloss
What are you talking about/talking of? you do talk of what?
Which person are you talking about/talking of? you do talk of what person?
of can also mean “from”, as we will see in the next section.

Coexistence with with

The word with is very straightforward. It is used just as with or and in English:
en gloss
The person is with the cat person do be with cat
Come with me! do go with me with!
I see a dog and a cat me do see big-good with cat
he mixed water with soil this back do with-do water with dirt
Note that English uses "with" to mean some things that are quite different, for example as the instrumental marker (indicating how you are using something to do something)! e.g.:
en gloss
he mixed water with a stick (he used a stick to mix water) this back do use plant-arm do with-do water
he mixed water with a stick (he and a stick were together mixing water, lol wat?) this back do with-do water with plant-arm

Basic Prepositions of location/movement

There are no true prepositions in Sesowi – everything is ultimately a noun. When you would use a preposition to describe where something is in English, just use the noun for corresponding location in Sesowi. If a dog is on the roof, you say that the dog is top the roof. If you are going to the store, you are going direction the store; and if you are going from the store, you are going of the store. If the dog jumps onto the roof, it jumps direction top the roof.

English has a lot of prepositions for how two objects can be related. The snake can be in front of the dog, near the dog, inside the dog, above the dog, on the dog, or by the side of the dog. Some of these change if there is movement: for instance, after the snake goes into the dog it is inside the dog. In Sesowi, these prepositions are simply expressed as one of the basic prepositions of movement (usually direction) plus the location relative to the object. Let’s look at a few examples. In these examples, we’ll break them up by whether the movement is static (happening in one location), or as a movement to- or from that location:
en gloss
She drove to the store this back do over-go direction give-metal-place
She drove from the store this back do over-go of give-metal-place
She drove through the store this back do over-go through give-metal-place
She was driving at the store this back do over-go place give-metal-place

Spatial prepositions

location noun: inside
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming inside the dog long-animal do water-go inside big-good static
the snake is swimming into the dog long-animal do water-go direction inside big-good movement
the snake is swimming out from the dog long-animal do water-go of inside big-good rev. mvmnt.
location noun: over
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming on/over the house long-animal do water-go over big-container static
the snake is swimming to the area on/over the house long-animal do water-go direction over big-container movement
the snake is swimming out from the area on/over the house long-animal do water-go of over big-container rev. mvmnt.
the snake is swimming over (through the space over) the house long-animal do water-go through over big-container through
location noun: surface. This shows how you can use any spatial location as a preposition:
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming on the sruface of the house long-animal do water-go surface big-container static
the snake is swimming onto the surface of the house long-animal do water-go direction surface big-container movement
the snake is swimming out from on the surface of the house long-animal do water-go of surface big-container rev. mvmnt.
location noun: small-distance
en gloss direction
the snake is swimming near the dog long-animal do water-go small-distance big-good static
the snake is swimming near to where the dog is long-animal do water-go direction small-distance big-good movement
the snake is swimming out from near where the dog is long-animal do water-go of small-distance big-good rev. mvmnt.
the snake is swimming past the dog long-animal do water-go through small-distance big-good through

Temporal prepositions

Prepositions of time are expressed with the same words as those of space. Imagine yourself as a box where the future stretches out in front of you and the past behind. Now imagine that you yourself are an event in time. You see another event in the future, in front of you. This event happens after you in time — so in this spatial metaphor, it happens in front of you. You happen before it — so, spatially, you happen behind it. Say there is another event that starts before you and ends after you. You happen during that event — spatially, you happen in it. And if you (the event) started at the same time as the other event and continue on forwards in time — aka you happen since that event — spatially, you happen starting at that event.1

Prepositions in verb phrases

Many Indo-European verbs have prepositions built into them, and it causes considerable chaos for learners to master them. In Sesowi, these prepositions are generally absent:
en gloss
I worked on a project me back do hard-do with-do
he depends on me this do fruit-want me
She believes in God this do truth-think over-truth-person
Look at this! you do see this with
what are you looking for? you do seek what?
They listen to music this number do sound-see good-sound
I am coping with loss. me do arm-have of-have
An exception is verbs that take indirect objects with the preposition to or about, like talk to or talk to. "to" is explained in the section on indirect objects, and "about" was explained in the of section above.

In some cases where English has different meanings for different prepositions, Sesowi has different verbs. In these cases you’ll notice that English also generally offers us easy synonyms:
en gloss
What are you looking at? you do see what thing?
What are you looking for? you do seek what thing?
What are youlooking up? you do seek what thing?
In other cases there is some ambiguity:
en gloss
She believes me this do truth-think me
She believes in God this do truth-think over-truth-person
She believes in me this do truth-think me
Notice however that even in English, believe in has a different meaning when applied to God versus to me! Really, there is ambiguity everywhere in all languages, but context clears almost everything up!
