Grammar 3: fitting the complexity of natural languages into the grammar you have just finished learning
Parts of speech
You may have noticed this already, but in Trevorese, most words are nouns. A verb is just “doing” a noun, and an adjective is just being “like” a noun. Therefore, there are only the following parts of speech:
  1. nouns (and pronouns)
    1. verbs: “do noun”
    2. modals: like verbs
    3. conjunctions: noun placed before clause
    4. prepositions: nouns placed before other nouns
    5. quantifiers: special nouns placed before other nouns (this/that/etc, “more”, “sixty-seven”…)
  2. Special markers: “do”, “like”

Other tenses with special Adverbs
The second important subtlety to using verbs well is adverbs. Let’s introduce a few very important adverbs:
en gloss
usually big-number-time
ever number-time
already back
just self
These allow us to express other tenses in various languages. Let’s look at them one-by-one: number-time is used for any habitual action: just use it as an adverb modifying the verb, e.g. <you normally go> “you do go like number-time”. In English, this is expressed with “used to” in the past tense, by the simple present tense or “usually” in the present, and by the progressive “-ing” in in the future:
en gloss
I am walking to school I do go-arm-go direction get-think-place
I [usually] walk to school I do go-arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place
I used to walk to school I back do go-arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place
I will be [regularly] walking to school I front do go-arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place
self is used exactly as the English “just”, to express a time very close to the present. However, English needs a lot of extra words to express this when combined with different tenses:
en gloss
I just walked to school I back do go-arm-go like swa direction get-think-place
I am just now walking to school I do go-arm-go like just direction get-think-place
I am just about to walk to school I do go-arm-go like just direction get-think-place
English has a tense called the perfect (“have”)1, as in the sentence “I have been to Germany” or “More people have been to Berlin than me”. All this is really conveying is that the action has, indeed, happened at least once, and likely many times, in contrast to a statement like “I went to Germany”, which just means you went a particular time. So in effect, the perfect is just saying that you went some time(s). At this point we introduce the Trevorese word time:
en gloss
I fell I back do down
I fell once I back do down like one times
I fell twice I back do down like two times
I fell 67 times I back do down like six-seven times
I fell several times I back do down like number times
If you use times without any quantifier, it just means “one or more times”:
en gloss
I fell some number of times I back do down like times
However, English has a different way of expressing this: the perfect tense! Hence:
en gloss
I have fallen I back do down like times
I have walked to school I back do go-arm-go like times direction get-think-place
I have walked to school twice I back do go-arm-go like two times direction get-think-place

Future Perfect Tense
The future perfect tense, or “past within future”, is a feature of Indo-European languages that occurs in dependent clauses. In Trevorese, it is simply expressed with the past:
en gloss
Next year we will have bought two dogs <Next year we did buy two dogs>
year like front me-number back do metal two of-back-go-animal
The context makes it clear that this “we did buy two dogs” is happening relative to the future, i.e. “next year”. Conversely, one can just as easily form the “future within past” tense!
en gloss
Last year we were going to buy two dogs <Last year we will buy two dogs> year like last number me will buy two of-back-go-animal
He said that he would sing <He said he will sing >
this back do say this will sing
The future-within-past in English has to be expressed with this rather cumbersome “were going to” construction, or the verb “would” which is ambiguous because “would” also has the separate meaning of the conditional mode. Past participles
The past participle in English expresses that a verb happened to some noun. It is a special way of making a verb into an adjective. In many cases, this is TODO TODO
  • me like stress
  • situation like stress
  • apple like fall
  • apple like eat like half
In Trevorese, this is treated no differently than any other adjective phrase: it is accomplished by a like phrase with the past tense:
en gloss
A fallen apple apple like this back do down
In English, past participles can also mean that “something did this”. In Trevorese, the construction is the same as before, but with a “some”:
en gloss
A half-eaten apple apple like [some] back do eat half [of this]
The words in brackets are optional; as in most languages, they can be left out. In English, past participles are a special construction only for the past participle of a verb. But in Trevorese, there is no difference between the past tense and other modals, so this same construction can be used for all the modals:
en gloss
An apple that is about to fall apple like [this] will down
An apple that is about to be eaten apple like [some] will eat [this]
An apple that can fall apple like [this] can down
An edible apple apple like [some] can eat [this]
An apple that may be edible apple like [some] may can eat [this]
Observe that English has all sorts of different constructions for these!
Counterfactuals

The Conditional and “if”

The conditional is a special tense (or “mood” if you’re a purist) that crops up in some languages. It’s basically identical to talking about things that would happen. In English, it’s most noticeable when it changes “will” into “would”, meaning “will go, conditional on that first statement”. This is simple enough in Trevorese: we just use case, which is identical to “if”. Essentially, it means “In the case that…”
en gloss
[future] if he sings, I will hear him case this front do sing, me front do sound-see this
[present] if he is singing, I hear him case this do sing, me do sound-see this
[past] if he were singing, I heard him case this back do sing, me back do sound-see this
Note: the “sing” in “if you sing, I will hear you”, even though it looks like the present tense, is actually in the future. Trevorese treats it as such.

The Subjunctive

The subjunctive happens when there is something that is happening counterfactually. For instance, in none of the sentences “If you were to come”, “If you had come”, and “I hope you come” did you actually come. Many languages, for instance Indo-European languages, have all sorts of complex ways of dealing with this situation. Trevorese, like Chinese, Telugu, Toki Pona, and so on, sidesteps this and leaves them ambiguous:
engloss
[future] if he sings, I will hear him
[future subj.] if he were to sing, I would hear him
deleteme
deleteme		 case this front do sing, me front do sound-see this
[present] if he is singing, I hear him
[present subj.] if he were singing, I would hear him
deleteme
deleteme		 case this do sing, me do sound-see this
[past] if he was singing, I heard him
[past subj.] if he had been singing, I would have heard him
[past subj.] if he had sung, I would have heard him
[past subj.] if he would of sung, I would have heard him		case this back do sing, me back do sound-see this
Note that the second part of the clause would be, in English, in the conditional. Conditional and subjunctive go together like bread and butter! But as we already established, there is no conditional in Trevorese, so the second half of the clause is just in normal indicative. Now, in some cases, the fact that the subjunctive and the indicative are the same may be annoying. The default interpretation is that we are talking about a subjunctive event. If we really need to clarify, we can add “in fact” to be clear that the statement is in the indicative:
engloss
[past] if he was singing, I heard him
[past subj.] if he had been singing, I would have heard him		case this back do sing, me back do sound-see this
[past] if he was in fact singing, I heard him
deleteme		case this back do sing like true, me back do sound-see this
“It”: Passive voice
German uses even more “it: than English, e.g. “It gonged”, “It is whispered in the ear”. Irish has its own conjugation, e.g. “déartear” – it is said. The passive is a somewhat unusual construction that crops up in some form or other in many languages. It can either be used to omit a subject, or to take responsibility away from them. Let’s look at the first example first. Any language needs some way to have sentences that don’t have a clear subject. Consider the following sentence: the milk was spilled All this is saying is that someone or something spilled the milk, but the speaker is not saying who. Fortunately, Trevorese has a pronoun for someone or something: some. Therefore, the passive can be expressed much more simply than in English:
en gloss
the milk was spilled some back do spill milk
someone or something spilled the milk some back do spill milk
Note English expresses this in the exact same way, if you care to express whether the doer was animate or inanimate, i.e. whether it was a person or thing. (TODO link that other chapter.) The same can be done in Trevorese:
en gloss
someone spilled the milk some person back do spill milk
something spilled the milk some thing back do spill milk
Now, what about the version of the passive that has a subject attached to it?
  • the milk was spilled by Fred
The only difference between this and the active voice is that it takes some responsibility away from Fred. This distinction doesn’t add much to the expressivity of the language, and can safely be removed. As such, Trevorese just uses the active voice in these constructions:
en gloss
the milk was spilled by Fred Fred back do spill cow-water
Fred spilled the milk Fred back do spill cow-water
Who was the milk spilled by? what person back do spill cow-water?
Who spilled the milk? what person back do spill cow-water?
*It is calling someone my name some person do call name of me
Her nose likes being kissed face-point of this do enjoy some do kiss [this]
Her nose likes being kissed by me face-point of this do enjoy me do kiss [this]
This last sentence is not grammatical in English, but it is normal in German!
“It”: Impersonal Aspect
Similar to the passive, the impersonal aspect is another way to avoid saying the subject. This comes up when there isn’t a subject, for instance for weather. With it is raining or it is dark, there is not really anything that the it refers to, excepting perhaps the universe itself. The only reason that “it” is added is because the grammar of the language demands some subject for the verb. In a language like Spanish, where subjects are not necessary, the subject is simply omitted: llueve means it rains. You can’t say *él llueve or something like that. Hebrew is the same (pro-drop). Although grammarians tend to think of the impersonal aspect as different from the passive voice, the authors of this work contend that they are actually the same. The only difference is that for the impersonal aspect, one is forced to omit the subject because there isn’t a subject, whereas for the passive, you choose to, because you don’t know who the subject is (or don’t want to say). What do we do in Trevorese? We just use this, in the sense of “this all is raining”. You can think of this use of “this” as someone gesturing in front of them and saying “all of this”. impersonal with verb:
en gloss
It is raining <this is doing rain>
this do rain
It is getting dark <this is doing dark>
this do dark
*It gongs [a gong noise is happening] this do gong
This last sentence is not grammatical in English, but it is normal in German! Impersonal with adjective:
en gloss
It is dark <this is dark>
this like dark
In some cases, using this may seem ambiguous, if there is already another this in the sentence, for instance a person. For instance, imagine we are talking about some guy:
en gloss
He walked into the room. It was dark. <this walked into room. this is dark>
It was raining as he walked. <this was raining as this walked.>
en gloss
It walked into the room. It was dark. <this walked into room. this is dark>
It was raining as it walked. <this was raining as this walked.>

There is / there are

“There is” is expressed just like the other impersonal pronouns, as “this do be”:
en gloss
There are a lot of people in the mall this do be big-number person in mall
