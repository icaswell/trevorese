Grammar 3: fitting the complexity of natural languages into the grammar you have just finished learning
Parts of speech

You may have noticed this already, but in Sesowi, all words are actually nouns. A verb is just “doing” (do) a noun, and an adjective is just being “like” (like) a noun. Let's look at all the different parts of speech for the atom place and front:

en gloss
[noun] the place place
[verb] He is putting a cat there. this do place cat that place
[adverb] He is putting a cat there. this do place cat that place
[preposition] He lives at the house this do life place big-container
en gloss
[noun] the front of the house front of big-container
[verb] He is facing me this do front me
[adjective] The front side of the house side like front of big-container
[modal] He will go this front do go
[conjunction] after he goes front this do go
Expressing other "tenses" with the power of time

What is verb "tense"? Well, "tense" just means "time", so it tells you about what time a verb was performed in. So at this point we introduce the Sesowi word time (time):

en gloss
I fell me back do down
I fell once me back do down like one time
I fell twice me back do down like two time
I fell 67 times me back do down like six-seven time
I fell several times me back do down like number time

If you use times without any quantifier, it just means “one or more times”:

en gloss
I fell some number of times me back do down like time

However, English has a different way of expressing this: the perfect tense! Hence:

en gloss
I have fallen me back do down like time
I have walked to school me back do arm-go like time direction get-think-place
I have walked to school twice me back do arm-go like two time direction get-think-place

The "perfect" tense is common in Indo-European languages, and uses the auxiliarly verb (“have”)1 to mark it, as in the sentence “I have been to Germany” or “More people have been to Berlin than me”. All this is really conveying is that the action has, indeed, happened at least once, and likely many times, in contrast to a statement like “I went to Germany”, which just means you went a particular time. So in effect, the perfect is just saying that you went some time(s). And that indeed is how the Sesowi expresses it, as we have just seen.

We have just learned how to express that something has happened at least once. What id you want to express that it is a habitual action, or has happened many times? Just use number-time (aka "multiple times")!

en gloss
I am walking to school me do arm-go direction get-think-place
I [usually] walk to school me do arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place
I used to walk to school me back do arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place
I will be [regularly] walking to school me front do arm-go like number-time direction get-think-place

Another thing you might want to express is something that happened in a time very close to the present, i.e. that has just happened or is just about to happen. This can be accomplished with self (self):

en gloss
I am just now walking to school me self do arm-go direction get-think-place
I just walked to school me self back do arm-go direction get-think-place
I am just about to walk to school me self front do arm-go direction get-think-place

English also has the curiously named tense, the pluperfect (the “had verbed” tense). This is specifically used for things that happened in the past and were already completed before another thing happened in the past — it’s kind of like “the past with respect to the past”. However, there is also a much simpler way of expressing the same thing in English: “already”. Sesowi takes this approach, with back:

en gloss
I had walked to school, but he didn’t know me back do arm-go direction get-think-place, against this no do hard-think
Future Perfect Tense

The future perfect tense, or “past within future”, is a feature of Indo-European languages that occurs in dependent clauses. In Sesowi, it is simply expressed with the past:

en loose gloss
Next year we will have bought two dogs <Next year we (already) bought two dogs> big-dirt-time like front me-number back do metal two big-good
We will have bought two dogs <We future bought two dogs> me-number front back do metal two big-good

The context makes it clear that this “we did buy two dogs” is happening relative to the future, i.e. “next year”. Conversely, one can just as easily form the “future within past” tense!

en loose gloss
Last year we were going to buy two dogs <Last year we will buy two dogs> big-dirt-time like back me-number front do metal two big-good
We were going to buy two dogs <We past will buy two dogs> me-number back front do metal two big-good
He said that he would sing <He said he will sing> this back do talk this front do good-sound
Next year we were going to buy two dogs, but we decided not to. <Next year we did will buy two dogs, but decide not to> big-dirt-time like front, me-number back front do metal two big-good, against me-number all-think no do

The future-within-past in English has to be expressed with this rather cumbersome “were going to” construction, or the verb “would” which is ambiguous because “would” also has the separate meaning of the conditional mode.

Participles

The past participle in English expresses that a verb happened to some noun. It is a special way of making a verb into an adjective. In Sesowi, this is treated no differently than any other adjective phrase:

en loose gloss
[past participle] A fallen apple <An apple that fell/falls> hot-fruit like do down
[present participle] A falling apple <An apple that falls now> hot-fruit like this-time do down
[adjective is often equivalent to p.p.] A fallen apple <An apple that fall> hot-fruit like down

The last form is the simplest and most natural -- it's just like saying "the apple that fall". However, in some cases this becomes ambiguous. For example, if you associate the word "eat" with an apple, you assume that means that this means that the apple is eaten, but if you associate it with a bear, you assume that it is the one doing the eating. If you need to be explicit, you can use the explicit form above, or the passive life construction:

en gloss
An eaten apple hot-fruit like eat
An eating apple (it's a living apple who's eating people!) hot-fruit like this do eat [person]
An eating bear hard-energy-animal like eat
An eaten bear hard-energy-animal like do life eat

In English, past participles are a special construction only for the past participle of a verb. But in Sesowi, there is no difference between the past tense and other modals, so this same construction can be used for all the modals:

en gloss
An apple that is about to fall hot-fruit like front do down
An apple that is about to be eaten hot-fruit like front do eat
An apple that can fall hot-fruit like can do down
An edible apple hot-fruit like can do eat
An apple that might be edible hot-fruit like possibility can do eat
[random] A half-eaten apple hot-fruit like one-part-two eat
[random] A half-eaten apple hot-fruit like thing back do eat one-part-two [of this]

Observe that English has all sorts of different constructions for these!

Counterfactuals

The Conditional and “if”

The conditional is a special tense (or “mood” if you’re a purist) that crops up in some languages. It’s basically identical to talking about things that would happen. In English, it’s most noticeable when it changes “will” into “would”, meaning “will go, conditional on that first statement”.

This is simple enough in Sesowi: we just use be, which is identical to “if”. Essentially, it means “In the case that…”

en gloss
[future] if he sings, I will hear him be this front do good-sound, me front do sound-see this
[present] if he is singing, I hear him be this do good-sound, me do sound-see this
[past] if he were singing, I heard him be this back do good-sound, me back do sound-see this

Note: the “sing” in “if you sing, I will hear you”, even though it looks like the present tense, is actually in the future. Sesowi treats it as such.

The Subjunctive

The subjunctive happens when there is something that is happening counterfactually. For instance, in none of the sentences “If you were to come”, “If you had come”, and “I hope you come” did you actually come. Many languages, for instance Indo-European languages, have all sorts of complex ways of dealing with this situation. Sesowi, like Chinese, Telugu, Toki Pona, and so on, sidesteps this and leaves them ambiguous:

en gloss
[future] if he sings, I will hear him
[future subj.] if he were to sing, I would hear him		 be this front do good-sound, me do sound-see this
[present] if he is singing, I hear him
[present subj.] if he were singing, I would hear him		 be this do good-sound, me do sound-see this
[past] if he was singing, I heard him
[past subj.] if he had been singing, I would have heard him
[past subj.] if he had sung, I would have heard him
[past subj.] if he would of sung, I would have heard him		 be this back do good-sound, me do sound-see this

Note that the second part of the clause would be, in English, in the conditional. Conditional and subjunctive go together like bread and butter! But as we already established, there is no conditional in Sesowi, so the second half of the clause is just in normal indicative.

Now, in some cases, the fact that the subjunctive and the indicative are the same may be annoying. The default interpretation is that we are talking about a subjunctive event. If we really need to clarify, we can add “in fact” to be clear that the statement is in the indicative:

en gloss
[past] if he was singing, I heard him
[past subj.] if he had been singing, I would have heard him		 be this back do good-sound, me back do sound-see this
[past] if in fact he was singing, I heard him be like truth this back do good-sound, me back do sound-see this
“It”: Passive voice

The passive is a somewhat unusual construction that crops up in some form or other in many languages. It can either be used to omit a subject, or to take responsibility away from them. Let’s look at the first example first. Irish has its own conjugation, e.g. “déartear” – it is said. German uses even more “it: than English, e.g. “It gonged”, “It is whispered in the ear”. Spanish, Hebrew, and others just leave off the subject from the sentence. In short: any language needs some way to have sentences that don’t have a clear subject. Consider the following sentence:

  • the milk was spilled

All this is saying is that someone or something spilled the milk, but the speaker is not saying who -- it's just someone or something. Sesowi uses a generic noun, equivalent to saying "Someone spilled the milk", but can also leave off the subject entirely:

en gloss
someone spilled the milk
the milk was spilled by someone		 person back do down light-water
something spilled the milk
the milk was spilled by something		 thing back do down light-water
The milk was spilled back do down light-water
The milk is spilling do down light-water

Now, what about the version of the passive that has a subject attached to it?

  • the milk was spilled by Fred

The only difference between this and the active voice is that it takes some responsibility away from Fred. This distinction doesn’t add much to the expressivity of the language, and can safely be removed. As such, Sesowi just uses the active voice in these constructions:

en gloss
the milk was spilled by Fred Fred back do down light-water
Fred spilled the milk Fred back do down light-water
Who was the milk spilled by? what person do down light-water?
Who spilled the milk? what person do down light-water?
Her nose likes being kissed nose of this do good-energy person do head-opening this
Her nose likes being kissed by me nose of this do good-energy me do head-opening this
*It is saying someone my name do talk self-talk of me

This last sentence is not grammatical in English, but it is normal in German!

There is however one more important way you can express this: you can use life to say that the noun has experienced the verb:
en loose gloss
Her nose is being kissed <Her nose experiences kisses> nose of this do life head-opening
Her nose was being kissed <Her nose experienced kisses> nose of this back do life head-opening
Her nose likes being kissed <Her nose likes to experience kisses> nose of this good-energy do life head-opening
Her nose likes being kissed by me <Her nose likes to experience my kisses> nose of this good-energy do life head-opening of me
“It”: Impersonal Aspect

Similar to the passive, the impersonal aspect is another way to avoid saying the subject. This comes up when there isn’t a subject, for instance for weather. With it is raining or it is dark, there is not really anything that the it refers to, excepting perhaps the universe itself. The only reason that “it” is added is because the grammar of the language demands some subject for the verb. In a language like Spanish, where subjects are not necessary, the subject is simply omitted: llueve means it rains. You can’t say *él llueve or something like that. Hebrew is the same (pro-drop).

Although grammarians tend to think of the impersonal aspect as different from the passive voice, the authors of this work contend that they are actually the same. The only difference is that for the impersonal aspect, one is forced to omit the subject because there isn’t a subject, whereas for the passive, you choose to, because you don’t know who the subject is (or don’t want to say).

What do we do in Sesowi? We can omit the subject, as in the passive, or use this, in the sense of “this all is raining”. You can think of this use of “this” as someone gesturing in front of them and saying “all of this”.

impersonal with verb:

en loose gloss
It is raining <this is doing rain> this do down-water
do down-water
It is getting dark <this is doing dark> this do dark
do dark
*It gongs [a gong noise is happening] <this is doing gong> this do flat-sound-metal
do flat-sound-metal

This last sentence is not grammatical in English, but it is normal in German!

The impersonal with adjectives is really the same, but let's give examples for good measure:

en loose gloss
It is dark <this is dark> this like dark
It is rainy <this is dark> this like down-water

In some cases, using this may seem ambiguous, if there is already another this in the sentence, for instance a person. For instance, imagine we are talking about some guy:

en gloss
He walked into the room. It was dark. <this walked into room. this is dark>
It was raining as he walked. <this was raining as this walked.>
en gloss
It walked into the room. It was dark. <this walked into room. this is dark>
It was raining as it walked. <this was raining as this walked.>

There is / there are

“There is” is expressed with have, as in Chinese

en gloss
There are a lot of people in the mall do have big-number person inside big-give-metal-place
