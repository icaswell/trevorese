Grammar Points

1. Simple Sentences with "du"

In Sesowi, a simple present tense sentence is formed with a subject, the verb marker du, and a verb root. The structure is Subject + du + Verb + (Object). The word bi acts like "to be" when talking about identity.

  • mi du bi mau. — I am a cat.
  • i du se. — It talks.

2. Negation with "no"

To make a sentence negative, you simply add no right before the verb marker du.

  • yu no du bi daga. — You are not a dog.
  • i no du se. — It does not talk.

Story: Mamang (A Friend)

i du bi mau. i du bi mamang. mi du bi daga. i du se. mi no du bi mau. yu du bi boi.

This is a cat. It is a friend. I am a dog. It talks. I am not a cat. You are an animal.

Grammar Points

1. Adjectives with "so"

In Sesowi, any noun can be used like an adjective. To modify a noun, you place the adjective-noun after it, connected by so. The structure is Noun + so + Adjective.

Think of so as meaning "who's/that's" or "like".

  • mau so niyang — A black cat. (lit. "cat that's like blackness")
  • daga so da — A big dog. (lit. "dog that's like bigness")

2. "To be" with Adjectives

To say "The cat is black", you use the same structure. The verb du bi is not needed for describing attributes. This construction can mean both "the black cat" and "the cat is black".

  • i mau so niyang. — This cat is black.
  • a daga no so wi. — That dog is not small.

Story: i dabau (This House)

i du bi dabau. a dabau so da. i du bi mau de mi. i so bai. i no so da. i so wi. i du bi mamang so ga. a daga so noga.

This is a house. That house is big. This is my cat. It is white. It is not big. It is small. It is a good friend. That dog is bad.

Grammar Points

1. Plurals are Optional

In Sesowi, the plural and singular forms of a noun are the same. mau can mean "a cat" or "cats".

To be specific, you can use quantifiers. wa means "one", making it unambiguously singular. na is a plural marker, making it unambiguously plural.

  • daga — dog / dogs (ambiguous)
  • wa daga — one dog (unambiguous singular)
  • na daga — dogs (unambiguous plural)
  • a na daga so da. — Those dogs are big.

2. Questions with "e"

The word e is a fundamental quantifier that means "what" or "which". At the end of a sentence, it turns it into a yes-no question.

  • yu du bi mamang e? — Are you a friend?
  • e daga so ga? — Which dog is good?

You can answer with dwe (yes/correct) or no (no).

Story: na boi (The Animals)

mi du si na boi. wa boi du bi mau so niyang. i du ka kagu. mi du si na daga so bai. ina du go lu so ga. e boi so da? a na daga so da. i mau so ga e? dwe.

I see animals. One animal is a black cat. It eats food. I see white dogs. They go to a good place. Which animal is big? Those dogs are big. Is this cat good? Yes.

Grammar Points

1. Past and Future Tenses

Tenses in Sesowi are simple. To mark the past tense, place ya (back) before du. To mark the future tense, place be (front) before du.

  • Present: mau du kogo. — The cat walks.
  • Past: mau ya du kogo. — The cat walked.
  • Future: mau be du kogo. — The cat will walk.

This also works with negation:

  • mau no ya du kogo. — The cat did not walk.

2. The Preposition "tu" (to)

The word tu is a core preposition meaning "to" or "towards". It indicates direction or a recipient.

  • mi be du go tu dabau. — I will go to the house.
  • i ya du se tu mi. — He talked to me.

Story: yabaitai (Yesterday)

yabaitai mi ya du kogo tu dabau de yu. mi ya du si na daga so wi. i daga de yu ya du subo lu so bai. bebaitai mi be du go tu dabau de yu. mi be du loka lo. yu no be du kumeng i ting e? no. yu no be du kumeng.

Yesterday I walked to your house. I saw small dogs. Your dog sat in a bright place. Tomorrow I will go to your house. I will drink water. You won't know this, will you? No. You will not know.

Grammar Points

1. Wh- Questions

The question word e (what/which) can combine with general nouns like mang (person) or lu (place) to form "Wh-" questions. These question words go where the answer would go in a statement.

  • yu du bi emang? — Who are you? (lit. You are what-person?)
  • i mau du go elu? — Where is the cat going? (lit. The cat is going what-place?)
  • yu du yau eting? — What do you want? (lit. You want what-thing?)

2. Commands with "ba"

To make a command, you add the particle ba to the end of a sentence. For direct commands, you can also omit the subject "you" (yu).

  • yu du se tu mau ba! — Talk to the cat!
  • du loka lo ba! — Drink water!

Story: elu du yo lo? (Where is there water?)

mi du yau logo. mi du se tu yu. "elu mi du logo e?" yu du se. "ewai yu du yau logo?" mi du se, "mi du yau si na loboi!" yu du se, "a lolu so da du bi lu so ga. du go ba!"

I want to swim. I say to you, "Where can I swim?" You say, "Why do you want to swim?" I say, "I want to see fish!" You say, "That big lake is a good place. Go!"

Grammar Points

1. Adverbs with "so"

Adverbs are formed the exact same way as adjectives. You use so after the verb to describe how the action is done.

  • i daga so gali du kogo. — The happy dog walks. (Adjective)
  • i daga du kogo so gali. — The dog walks happily. (Adverb)
  • i du se so kwi. — He talks quickly.

2. Modal Verbs

Modal verbs like "can" or "must" come before the main verb's du marker. Only the main verb gets a du. The structure is Subject + Modal + du + Verb.

  • i nang du go. — It can go. / It may go.
  • mi dwa du kudu. — I must work. / I should work.
  • yu no dwa du se so nogali. — You should not speak sadly.
  • i be nang du pumeng. — It will be able to study.

Story: kudu (Work)

mi dwa du kudu. mi du kudu so nokwi. ewai? mi so nogali. mamang de mi ya du se tu mi. "yu dwa du bi so gali ba!" mi be nang du kudu so kwi e? dwe. bebaitai mi be du kudu so gali.

I must work. I work slowly. Why? I am sad. My friend said to me, "You should be happy!" Will I be able to work quickly? Yes. Tomorrow I will work happily.

Grammar Points

1. Comparative with "mai"

To compare two things, use the word mai (more) before the adjective. The thing being compared to is introduced with tu (to).

  • a mau so mai da tu i mau. — That cat is bigger than this cat.
  • i lapa so gengmai swi tu a lapa. — This apple is less sweet than that apple.

2. Superlative with "ta"

To say something is the "most" of something, use the word ta (top/over) before the adjective. Often it is combined with mai for clarity.

  • i kiboi so ta mai wi. — This bird is the smallest. (lit. This bird is top-more small)
  • a gasang so ta ga. — That song is the best. (lit. That song is top good)

Story: na mau (The Cats)

i du bi mau de mi. i so yokang. a du bi mau de yu. i so yokang. geng, mau de mi so mai yokang tu mau de yu. laiting, mau de mi so da mai da! mau de yu du ka loboi. mau de mi du ka kiboi. mi du kumeng kiboi so mai ga tu loboi. pauting, mau de mi so ta ga.

This is my cat. He is rich. That is your cat. She is rich. But, my cat is richer than your cat. Also, my cat is much bigger! Your cat eats fish. My cat eats birds. I know birds are better than fish. In the end, my cat is the best.
